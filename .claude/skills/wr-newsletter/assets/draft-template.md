# Draft template: The AI Engineering Brief

Use this structure when drafting a brief. Fill every placeholder in square brackets.

## Voice reminders

- Team voice: "we", not "I" (ADR 010).
- Direct, specific, confident.
- No em-dashes. Use commas, periods, colons, or parentheses.
- Each item: one sentence per row. Concrete verbs, specific nouns, verifiable claims.

## Structure

```markdown
# The AI Engineering Brief: [WEEK ENDING YYYY-MM-DD]

[ONE-SENTENCE INTRO stating the theme of this week's items. Example: "Three stories about what engineering teams are having to relearn."]

### Item 1: [HEADLINE, 6 TO 10 WORDS]

- What happened: [ONE SENTENCE. Factual. Name the org, name the artifact, name the date if relevant.]
- Why it matters to your team: [ONE SENTENCE. Operational consequence. "Your team now needs to..." or "This changes how..."]
- The human angle: [ONE SENTENCE. Change management, psychology, hiring, morale, or organisational consequence.]
- Source: [URL]

### Item 2: [HEADLINE]

- What happened: ...
- Why it matters to your team: ...
- The human angle: ...
- Source: [URL]

### Item 3: [HEADLINE]

- What happened: ...
- Why it matters to your team: ...
- The human angle: ...
- Source: [URL]

---

We help engineering teams navigate this. windyroad.com.au
```

## CTA variants (pick one or write a new one in the same tone)

- "We help engineering teams navigate this. windyroad.com.au"
- "We help engineering leaders stay patch fit as the pace of change picks up. windyroad.com.au"
- "We work with teams that need to move faster without breaking things. windyroad.com.au"

## What NOT to do

- Do not write speculative claims without attribution. If uncertain, omit the item.
- Do not use hype words ("revolutionary", "game-changing", "unprecedented").
- Do not pad. Three items is the format. Two is acceptable if three candidates did not clear the filter.
- Do not link-dump. Every source link should have a one-sentence "What happened" anchoring it.
