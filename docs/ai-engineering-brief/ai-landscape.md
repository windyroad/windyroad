## Analysis

### Differentiation

Shipped Product is supported by two high-visibility dependencies on the map: Engineering Team Capability and Delivery Pipeline. Both are what the reader's organisation pays for; everything lower on the map is substrate that feeds them. This matters because an AI Coding Assistants choice is effectively an Engineering Team Capability decision, and a Delivery Pipeline choice determines where AI Security Tooling hooks in. Read the custom components below with that chain in mind: what looks like deep-substrate tooling shapes the two top-visibility components the business depends on.

AI Security Tooling sits at Genesis (0.12), Model Evaluation Frameworks at early Custom-Built (0.22, shifted from 0.18 this week), and AI Agent Runtimes (0.28, shifted from 0.22), AI Regulation and Compliance (0.28, shifted from 0.20), Engineering Team Capability (0.25), and Prompt and Context Management (0.28) all sit in the Custom-Built band. These six are where durable advantage is still available to engineering leaders because none of them are buy-off-the-shelf yet. AI Agent Runtimes is a concentration risk: four downstream dependencies (Foundation Models, Model Hosting and Inference, Prompt and Context Management, Model Evaluation Frameworks) rely on it, so a runtime choice that cannot be reversed amplifies every other bet; this week's convergence on standards (OpenAI Agents SDK v2, NIST standards initiative, Spec-Kit frameworks) narrows the window to make that choice consciously. The investment that compounds fastest is Engineering Team Capability paired with Model Evaluation Frameworks: better evaluation creates the feedback loop that grows team capability, and capable teams build evaluation that targets the right failure modes; reassess this pairing when a vendor evaluation product covers the current use-case set for less than six months of internal development cost, or when quarterly evaluation-run volume falls below one per AI-assisted change. Prompt and Context Management and AI Agent Runtimes are worth revisiting quarterly as the ecosystem standardises. Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34 named "Codebase cognitive debt" as a Caution this quarter, which is the same class of risk as concentration on an unreversible agent-runtime pick: teams that adopt AI-generated patterns without understanding them compound a debt that only surfaces at maintenance. **What to do:** treat Engineering Team Capability and Model Evaluation Frameworks as the two budget lines that never lose funding this year; defer Prompt and Context Management build-out until the second standardisation signal lands.

### Evolution

Seven components have active evolve arrows; five moved visibly this week. AI Coding Assistants (Product, 0.55 evolving toward 0.78) continue to converge across Cursor, Copilot, Claude Code, and open-weight tools, with Simon Willison observing that a locally-run Qwen3.6-35B model produced better output than Claude Opus 4.7 on his specific test, which means team skill composition differentiates more than tool choice. Foundation Models (Product, 0.60 evolving toward 0.80) are commoditising faster than last week under open-weight pressure: Google Gemma 4 runs natively on iPhone with full offline inference, putting a capable open model in the same class as the frontier closed models. AI Agent Runtimes (now Custom-Built, 0.28 evolving toward 0.50) moved significantly: OpenAI released Agents SDK v2, Cloudflare launched an inference platform explicitly designed for agents, NIST announced an AI Agent Standards Initiative for interoperability, and Thoughtworks placed OpenSpec and GitHub Spec-Kit in Trial, all inside the same week. Model Evaluation Frameworks (Custom-Built, 0.22 evolving toward 0.42) moved on credible benchmark arrivals: UK AISI's SandboxEscapeBench for measuring container escape by agents and its analysis across 177,000 AI agent tools give teams a pattern to copy rather than invent. Prompt and Context Management (Custom-Built, 0.28 evolving toward 0.45) is evolving in parallel to Agent Runtimes as standardisation mechanisms (OpenSpec, Agent Skills, Spec-Kit) codify context-handling as a distinct concern. AI Security Tooling (Genesis, 0.12 evolving toward 0.28) has a sustained evolve arrow even though the position did not move this week: the UK AISI's shift toward defence-leaning initiatives and the Sanfilippo critique of activity-without-protection products both point to a future where AI security tooling earns real differentiation, but the maturation signal is still nascent. AI Regulation and Compliance (Custom-Built, 0.28 evolving toward 0.48) also moved this week: the EU AI Act moved into Chapter V enforcement, a US district court ruled in US v. Heppner that AI chat logs lack attorney-client privilege, and the Australian OAIC published transparency guidance for automated decision-making; each is enforceable today, not a roadmap item. **What to do:** set up a quarterly budget review that rebalances integration investment against assistant-swap and runtime-swap readiness; watch the second agent-standard to land as the signal that runtime lock-in is crystallising.

### Risk

External vendor exposure: when a dominant coding-assistant vendor changes its default model or pricing tier, integrations that reach directly into the vendor need rewriting while the week's work slips, and the fallback is an abstraction layer in front of the assistant before consolidation arrives. Commodity layer: Model Hosting and Inference (Commodity, 0.70) could change terms; trigger is a hosting provider announcing a deprecation or a pricing change above 20%, affected components are AI Coding Assistants and AI Agent Runtimes, and the fallback is a multi-provider abstraction paired with a contract termination review. Regulatory shift: trigger is an AU, EU, or US regulator naming a concrete practice that must stop or start (example from this week: the US v. Heppner ruling means AI chat logs are now discoverable in US federal litigation, which changes retention and logging defaults for every engineering team doing AI-assisted customer or legal work), and without an inventory of AI-assisted code and AI chat interactions, the time to answer a regulator is measured in months not hours. Internal evaluation cadence: teams that pause model evaluation for AI-assisted changes see silent quality drift set in within weeks as the feedback loop stops producing signal, and the trigger to watch is any four-week stretch with no evaluation run; this is the only risk on the map engineering leaders directly control. Codebase cognitive debt (Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34 Caution this week): teams that adopt AI-generated code patterns without understanding them incur hidden maintenance debt that compounds, and the trigger is any pull request where the author cannot explain the pattern's tradeoffs on review. **What to do:** build the AI-assisted-code inventory this quarter (the regulatory lever means this is no longer optional), set an evaluation-run watchdog that alerts on four-week gaps, and add a "can you explain the pattern?" question to the pull-request template.

### Decisions

AI Evaluation Framework investment and AI Agent Runtime investment compete for the same engineering time, and this week's convergence on agent standards narrows the reassess window. Build a shared evaluation harness when three internal teams have asked for one in the last quarter, because evaluation feeds every other AI-assisted decision downstream; reassess in six months if a vendor product covers the full evaluation use-case set for less than six months of internal development cost. If twelve months pass with no internal team requesting shared evaluation, revisit whether AI-assisted change is happening at a scale that justifies the framework.

## How this map is used

The Engineering Leader persona (see `docs/JOBS_TO_BE_DONE.md`) is the reader this map serves: responsible for delivery pipeline, security posture, and team capability, stack-agnostic, and credential-sensitive.

1. The `/wr-newsletter` skill reads this file before drafting each weekly brief. The current landscape is the context the skill uses to decide which stories represent real movement versus surface noise, and to write the "map movement" line that appears on each brief item.
2. After drafting, the skill proposes updates to `ai-landscape.owm` for any component that moved this week, any new component that emerged, or any dependency that shifted. The update is captured in git so the week-to-week delta is visible.
3. Tom refines positions whenever he disagrees with the skill's placement. Edit `ai-landscape.owm` by hand, then re-render via the `wr-wardley:generate` skill (pointed at this OWM file) or by invoking its `owm-to-svg.mjs` converter directly. The skill command is the canonical path; the direct converter is an escape hatch for fast iteration.

## Known limitations of v1

- AI Regulation and Compliance sits at 0.60 visibility, which is high. Debatable: regulation is mostly invisible until it isn't. The week's regulatory movements (EU enforcement, US legal ruling, OAIC guidance) argue for keeping it visible; adjust downward if the run of regulatory news subsides.
- No evolve arrow on Compute and GPU Infrastructure or on the anchor-adjacent components. Compute is already commodity and does not need one; the anchor components are stable at their current positions.
- Source coverage this week was partial: OpenAI direct page returned 403 (covered via Google News RSS), Reddit was blocked by the tool chain (r/LocalLLaMA and r/MachineLearning lost), US FTC and OECD endpoints failed.
