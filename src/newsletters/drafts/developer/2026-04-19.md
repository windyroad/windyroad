# Opus 4.7 shipped. Qwen beat it on a laptop. Now technique wins.

*Tokens Spent, AI engineering for developers, week ending 2026-04-19*

About Tokens Spent: each issue cuts the week in AI tooling down to what is worth your time. Items label their evidence as shipped, benchmarked, demo, or not yet. The default verdict is skip; items here earned their slot. Published weekly, Wednesday morning, by Windy Road.

**From Tom**

Two weeks ago Thoughtworks put Spec-Driven Development at Trial. I keep reading that as good news and flinching. Yes, document what you want the coding agent to build. No, do not write the spec up front and hand it over, because that is Big Up-Front Design in a new costume. We have forty years of evidence that waterfall delivers what we asked for, not what we wanted. Gall's Law says a complex working system evolved from a simple working one. Spec-first violates that.

What seems to work is iterative documentation the agent itself maintains as you go. The Windy Road JTBD plugin is our experiment in this; [wr-architect](https://github.com/windyroad/windyroad) is the same pattern one layer up, recording ADRs that anchor future decisions. Neither is the right answer. Both are the right shape: document as a byproduct of working, not as a gate before it. This week everything shipped at once, which makes this the week where technique matters more than tool choice.

**Net movement this week:**
  - Coding assistants commodified further.
  - Local open-weight models crossed a visible parity line on Simon Willison's eval.
  - Agent-runtime platforms agreed on shape; per-agent-hour costs kept rising.
  - Agentic-engineering practices earned their first Trial-ring placement on the Thoughtworks Radar.

### Item 1: Opus 4.7, Codex-for-almost-everything, and Claude Design all shipped inside three days

**Evidence stance:** shipped.

**What happened:**
  - Anthropic released [Claude Opus 4.7](https://anthropic.com/news/claude-opus-4-7) on 2026-04-16 with stronger coding, agent, vision, and multi-step performance.
  - Anthropic Labs launched [Claude Design](https://anthropic.com/news/claude-design-anthropic-labs) on 2026-04-17, a collaborative-design surface.
  - OpenAI shipped the Codex expansion covered in the AI Daily Brief piece [How to Use Opus 4.7 and the New Codex](https://aidailybrief.beehiiv.com/p/how-to-use-opus-4-7-and-the-new-codex), under the tag "Codex for almost everything".
  - Simon Willison shipped [llm-anthropic 0.25](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/16/llm-anthropic/) with Opus 4.7 support and extended thinking the day after release.

**Why it matters to you:** this is not the week to try the new thing. It is the week to ask what you are committing to when every vendor ships a month's roadmap in three days. Specific capabilities matter more than the aggregate noise. Codex rate-card visibility changes whether you can justify running it on PR review. Claude Design is a net-new surface, not a faster version of something you already used. Treat each as a separate adoption decision, not a bundle.

**The human angle:** every tool shipped this week came with a migration tax. The tools that pay that tax back fastest are the ones your team was already using. The rest we should not adopt just because the vendor shipped them.

### Item 2: A laptop beat Opus 4.7 on Simon Willison's eval. Start experimenting with local models.

**Evidence stance:** shipped (Simon ran both locally).

**What happened:**
  - Simon Willison posted [Qwen3.6-35B-A3B on my laptop drew me a better pelican than Claude Opus 4.7](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/16/qwen-beats-opus/) on 2026-04-16, with side-by-side outputs.
  - Google DeepMind shipped [Gemma 4](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/gemma-4/), framed as "byte for byte the most capable open models".

**Why it matters to you:** the tokens-spent calculus is genuinely shifting. For tasks where a 35B local model wins on your eval, paying cloud tokens is a tax on nothing. You do not have to migrate everything to local inference this quarter. You do have to spend one evening this month setting up a local-model workflow so you can test before you commit next quarter.

**Minimum-risk path:**
  - Run Ollama or llama.cpp on the machine you already have.
  - Pull a Qwen3.6 or Gemma 4 weight.
  - Point your existing eval prompts at it.
  - Compare outputs to whatever cloud model you use today.

If local wins on even one recurring task, you have a real choice that was not on the table six months ago.

**The human angle:** running a model on your own hardware changes how much you trust the output. Cloud inference feels like a vendor promise; local inference feels like a hammer you own. That second feeling is the one developers under-weight.

**Source:**
  - https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/16/qwen-beats-opus/
  - https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/gemma-4/

### Item 3: The agent-runtime layer commodified this week. The unit economics did not.

**Evidence stance:** shipped infrastructure plus benchmarked cost analysis.

**What happened:**
  - Cloudflare launched an [AI Platform inference layer designed for agents](https://blog.cloudflare.com/ai-platform/) (306 points on Hacker News).
  - Toby Ord published [Are the costs of AI agents also rising exponentially?](https://www.tobyord.com/writing/hourly-costs-for-ai-agents) with a cost-curve analysis (292 points).
  - Tom Tunguz wrote [The beginning of scarcity in AI](https://tomtunguz.com/ai-compute-crisis-2026/) on compute constraints (186 points).

**Why it matters to you:** most readers of this newsletter are not building agents right now. You are shipping regular features with coding assistants doing 30-60% of the typing. That is fine.

When the agent conversation reaches you, ask first whether the feature needs a full agent or a coding-assistant-with-a-tight-scope. This week's cost data helps that decision. Agent-hour costs are climbing faster than the cost of running the same work as a scoped assistant call. The platforms will agree on shape this year. The price per agent-hour will keep rising for longer. Pick an agent framework this quarter and you will likely pick a platform that survives. You will also lock in a bill that keeps climbing.

**The human angle:** "we need an agent for that" is becoming the 2026 version of "we need a microservice for that". Most of the time the answer is neither.

  - https://www.tobyord.com/writing/hourly-costs-for-ai-agents

### Item 4: Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34 put spec-driven development at Trial. Read it carefully.

**Evidence stance:** shipped at multiple Thoughtworks client organisations (Trial ring).

**What happened:**
  - Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34 placed [Spec-Driven Development, Agent Skills, Feedback Sensors for Coding Agents, and Measuring Collaboration Quality with Coding Agents](https://www.thoughtworks.com/radar) at Trial.
  - Radar also placed [Codebase Cognitive Debt](https://www.thoughtworks.com/radar) and Agent Instruction Bloat at Assess.

**Why it matters to you:** this is the first time a mainstream advisory body has named agentic-engineering practices as multi-client production material. Trial-ring placement is signal that these practices work somewhere. It is not signal that they work the way they are being marketed.

Spec-Driven Development as "write the spec, hand it to the agent, get the feature" is waterfall in new clothes. It inherits waterfall's failure mode: you get what you asked for, not what you wanted. The practices that survive in the Windy Road codebase run iteratively and treat documentation as an anchor the agent maintains, not a gate before it starts. Try Agent Skills and Feedback Sensors this quarter. Read Spec-Driven critically rather than adopting it whole.

**The human angle:** every wave of process tooling gets sold on the promise that this time the up-front model is right. It rarely is. Gall's Law still applies: a complex working system evolved from a simple working one. Your job is to keep the iteration loop tight; the documentation is a byproduct.

**Source:**
  - https://www.thoughtworks.com/radar

**Evidence stance:** shipped reality.

**What happened:**
  - On 2026-04-15, Sam Altman's residence was attacked as part of a broader wave of public backlash against AI industry figures. AI Daily Brief covered the context in ["AI Populism Turns Violent"](https://aidailybrief.beehiiv.com/p/ai-populism-turns-violent) the same day, and their take is more mature than ours can be in one paragraph, so read theirs.

**Why it matters to you:** an incident this week involving a named industry figure is the kind of signal that changes what shipping AI features into human-facing surfaces looks like in context. The AI Daily Brief's piece is the mature read on it. Ours is narrower: the work that earns adoption on any AI rollout, at any scale, is the analysis before the build. Listen to the people the feature will affect. Engage them through delivery. Measure adoption on signals beyond raw usage. This is part of the feature, not a sidebar.

**The human angle:** addressing perceived inequality is the work, and it starts with the people closest to you and the features you are shipping this quarter, not with the industry-level debate.
