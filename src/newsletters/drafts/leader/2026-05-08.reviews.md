## Post-publish substance correction (2026-05-08, after gates passed)

After all four finalise gates returned PASS, Tom flagged a substantive editorial error in Item 1 that none of the gates caught. The original framing ("what model are we using is becoming a security review question"; "List every place your team uses an LLM... Cross-check against the AISI capability tier") implied the threat was the team's own deployed models going rogue and performing cyber-attacks. The actual finding from AISI is the inverse: bad actors with access to frontier models can now run multi-step end-to-end cyber-attacks at a verified capability tier. The threat is attackers using these models against your perimeter, not your own models attacking you.

Item 1's Why-it-matters, Human angle, and Try-this were rewritten to reflect the threat-model framing (update the assumed attacker capability tier; confirm pen-test currency; audit detection-and-response against multi-step LLM-driven attack chains; surface to whoever owns threat modelling). Item 2's "alongside the AISI capability tier from Item 1" line was rewritten to clarify the two are different surfaces (own-agent exfiltration versus external-attacker capability). Opener bridge paragraph and LinkedIn post bullet 1 were updated to match.

This is a notable rubric gap: voice gate, content-risk gate, sw-critic, and editor all PASSED on the wrong threat-model framing. None of the rubrics test for "does the brief have the threat direction right when discussing attacker capabilities". Worth a follow-up rubric extension. Captured here as the audit trail for the post-PASS substance correction; the body of the brief now reflects the corrected framing.
