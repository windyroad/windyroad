# Issue 09: Own your AI supply before someone else switches it off

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-06-14*

From Tom: I keep coming back to one idea. The IT department is usually the limiting factor on what a business can build, and AI lifts that limit. The more we can build, the more we want to build, and in some cases the demand that unlocks is the financial case for more capacity, not less. I read this week's news through that lens. The headline is genuinely alarming, a government switched two frontier models off for foreign users overnight and a competitor's lobbying was reportedly part of how it happened. The lesson underneath is old and boring: keep your options open. Put a self-hostable open-weight model in your evaluation set now, not because it is the best model, but because it is the one nobody can switch off. Google's Gemma 4 12B, open and non-Chinese, is the candidate I would start with. The throughline this week is optionality.

### Item 1: A government switched two frontier models off overnight

**What happened.** The US administration issued a directive that forced Anthropic to suspend foreign access to its two most capable models, Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5. Anthropic [published a statement confirming it complied](https://www.anthropic.com/news/fable-mythos-access) (Jun 12). The Wall Street Journal reported that [Amazon CEO discussions with US officials triggered the crackdown](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/amazon-ceos-talks-with-u-s-officials-triggered-crackdown-on-anthropic-models-dcc90578) (Jun 13), so a commercial rival's lobbying was reportedly part of how the decision was made.

**What shifted.** Federal involvement in AI crossed a line this week. A month ago the government was talking about revenue and equity participation. This week it issued a directive that executed, cutting specific models off for a class of users overnight. The relationship is hardening from "government as stakeholder" to "government as an operating control point in the model supply chain."

**Why it matters to your team.** The procurement question is no longer only "is this vendor financially stable?" It is now "can my government, my vendor's government, or my vendor's largest competitor cause this model to disappear from my stack?" That is a continuity question, and the cheapest answer is portability. A self-hostable open-weight model, already evaluated and ready to run, is a continuity hedge, not only a cost hedge. We have made this case before: our [Issue 03](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/six-ai-shifts-week-all-them-measurement-problems-tom-howard-maicd-9ntxc/) walked through evaluating open-weight models on real workloads before you need them, and the off-switch is exactly the scenario that makes it worth doing now. Google [shipped Gemma 4 12B as an open multimodal model](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/introducing-gemma-4-12b/) this week, which makes the non-Chinese open option more credible than it was a quarter ago.

**The human angle.** Your team did not cause this and cannot fix it, but they will carry it if access vanishes mid-project. Naming a fallback model now is a way to spend a calm afternoon on something that would otherwise be a bad week.

### Item 2: AI keeps adding capacity, and capacity is a demand engine

**What happened.** The Wall Street Journal ran ["The Job That AI Was Supposed to Kill Needs More Humans Than Ever"](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/the-job-that-ai-was-supposed-to-kill-needs-more-humans-than-ever-0771e4cf) (Jun 13), documenting roles that were predicted to shrink and are instead hiring. Bloomberg ran [the inverse anxiety](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/ai-turns-coders-lawyers-analysts-105500495.html) (Jun 14) the same week, framing experienced professionals as feeling like "ghosts." Both can be true at once: more roles overall, and a harder road for individuals who do not adapt. The aggregate signal is the one that should drive a headcount plan.

**What shifted.** Nothing fundamental moved this week, but the counter-narrative to AI-replaces-headcount picked up two heavyweight datapoints. The pattern we have tracked since [Issue 06](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/issue-06-ais-real-prize-capacity-smaller-team-tom-howard-maicd-uabmc/) holds: the prize is capacity, not a smaller team.

**Why it matters to your team.** This is our read, not a settled fact: the AI-headcount calculus is not a clean substitution. When AI lifts the engineering-capacity constraint that usually limits what a business can build, latent demand surfaces against the new capacity. In some cases that surfaced demand is the financial case for more capacity, not less. Plan team structure around augmentation and the new review-and-oversight load, not a replacement spreadsheet. The teams that win here point their extra capacity at demand they could not previously reach. The WSJ piece is the concrete signal worth reading for yourself before you commit to a headcount plan built on what the tools can do.

**The human angle.** "We will need fewer of you" and "we can finally build the backlog we never had capacity for" are very different messages to carry into a team. The second is closer to what we see in the evidence, and it is the one that keeps good engineers in the room.

### Item 3: The rules that bind you fragmented in two directions at once

**What happened.** State attorneys general [opened investigations into OpenAI](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/13/technology/states-investigating-openai.html) (Jun 13). In the same week the White House and Hill [relaunched an effort to preempt state AI laws](https://www.yahoo.com/news/politics/articles/scoop-white-house-hill-relaunch-222606064.html) (Jun 8), and Governor DeSantis publicly refused to cede ground. The rules are splitting at the same time the federal government is trying to consolidate them.

**What shifted.** This is the same federal-engagement story as Item 1, seen from the rules side. The direction is contested in public, which means the binding answer for your organisation is unsettled rather than arriving.

**Why it matters to your team.** Contract language that assumes a single federal standard is coming may be betting on an outcome that is actively being fought. Lead with that: review any AI vendor contract or compliance plan that quietly assumes federal preemption, because the preemption push and the state-level resistance are both live. "Which AI rules bind us" now has a moving, jurisdiction-dependent answer. If you operate across states or countries, your compliance posture has to hold more than one rulebook at once. The EU AI Act Article 50 transparency rules are the concrete fixed point in the noise: they still carry an August 2026 deadline, so they are the one date you can plan around today.

**The human angle.** This ambiguity lands on whoever owns compliance, usually a small number of people who cannot make the uncertainty go away. Giving them explicit permission to plan for divergence, rather than a single answer, is the kinder and the safer call.

### Item 4: AI-enabled attack is now a named, framework-mapped class

**What happened.** Anthropic and MITRE [published a year of AI-enabled cyber threats mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework](https://www.anthropic.com/news/AI-enabled-cyber-threats-mitre-attack), a widely used catalogue of attacker techniques (Jun 3). In the same week, Bloomberg reported [a crypto token lost roughly half its value to an AI-assisted hack](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-14/crypto-token-s-50-wipeout-shows-magnitude-of-ai-hacking-threat) (Jun 14), an early sign that AI-assisted attacks are already causing real financial damage somewhere.

**What shifted.** AI-enabled attack moved from "watch this space" to a named, framework-mapped class. ATT&CK is a language your security team already speaks, so the threat is now describable in terms they can act on rather than a vague warning.

**Why it matters to your team.** You can ask a concrete question this quarter: are we testing against the AI-enabled techniques in this mapping? The MITRE mapping turns an open-ended worry into a finite list you can prioritise. Hand your security lead the mapping and the question, and let them tell you where the gaps are.

**The human angle.** Security teams are tired of being told to worry about everything. A framework-mapped threat class is the opposite of that, a finite set of techniques to check. That is more respectful of their time than another open-ended alarm.

### Also worth noting

Both [Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/news/confidential-draft-s1-sec) and [OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/openai-submits-confidential-s-1/) confirmed draft S-1 filings this week, the registration step before a US stock-market listing. The two largest model providers are preparing to go public in the same window a government showed it can reach into their model access, which is the continuity question in Item 1 from a different angle.
