# Issue 11: Frontier AI is now a permission, not a purchase

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-06-28*

From Tom: Ford spent this year quietly rehiring the veteran inspectors it had handed over to AI. I have a lot of sympathy and one strong opinion. The mistake was never trying AI on the job. The mistake was putting it in place for everyone and then hoping for the best. That is lazy, it is high risk, and honestly it is poor leadership. The truth is that no one really knows which jobs AI does well and which it is not suitable for yet, and in what situations. So the only responsible move is to test and learn, in a safe way, before you bet a production line or a release on it. That is the thread running through this week. The model itself is the easy part. The judgment about where to trust it, and whether you are even allowed to run it, is the part you cannot buy.

Last week we argued the model is the easy part. This week showed what the hard part is: control of the model is no longer something you buy along with it. Three stories, one pattern. Permission to run a model, proof it works for your job, and ownership of what it can do all became open questions in the same seven days.

### Item 1: Frontier model access is now a government permission

**What happened.** This week the US government showed it can both grant and withhold access to a frontier model, and used both powers at once. It eased the foreign-access ban on Anthropic, [clearing Claude Mythos 5 for "trusted" US organisations](https://www.semafor.com/article/06/27/2026/us-releases-powerful-anthropic-model-mythos-to-some-us-companies) as the two sides moved toward a deal. In the same days OpenAI [released only a limited preview of its next model, GPT-5.6 Sol](https://openai.com/index/previewing-gpt-5-6-sol/), [holding back the full launch while the government took early access](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/openai-defers-public-rollout-gpt56-us-seeks-early-access-frontier-ai-models-2026-06-26/) for a security review. A US lawmaker also [introduced a bill](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/us-lawmaker-proposes-bill-require-ai-companies-report-critical-incidents-2026-06-25/) that would require AI companies to report critical incidents.

**What shifted.** Two weeks ago this was a single off-switch. Last week it was a negotiation. This week it became a working permission system. The same government granted access to one vendor and restricted it for another within days. That makes the off-switch a lever it can pull again, on any vendor, in either direction, not a one-time act. Model access is no longer a property of the model. It is a permission, and a permission can be revoked as easily as it was granted. One sign of the pressure this creates: over the weekend Austria [lobbied the EU to host Anthropic after the US access curbs](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-28/austria-lobbies-eu-to-host-anthropic-after-us-access-curbs), an early push for sovereign-hosted access in Europe that could, in time, give European teams a path the US does not control.

**Why it matters to your team.** A vendor contract will not save you here. A continuity clause with your supplier does nothing when a government, not the supplier, is the one switching the model off. That is the off-switch that opened this story two weeks ago. The move that helps is owning an alternative. Keep one capable open-weight model that you can run yourself in your evaluation set, tested against a real workload, so a directive against your primary vendor does not strand a project mid-flight. Stand it up now, because the trigger is already live, not next quarter.

**The human angle.** The people who carry this are whoever owns your vendor relationships, and they cannot make the uncertainty disappear. Let them stand up and test the fallback while the pressure is off, rather than improvising one in the week access changes without warning.

### Item 2: Ford rehired the inspectors it had given to AI

**What happened.** That Ford reversal is [on the record](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/ford-has-been-rehiring-quality-inspectors-after-ai-fell-short): the carmaker has been rehiring experienced quality inspectors after its AI inspection systems fell short on the line.

**What shifted.** This is the clearest production example yet: AI failed to replace a capability that proved harder to substitute than it looked. One company's reversal does not change how routine that underlying skill is across the industry, but it does sharpen the lesson. The expensive part was not that Ford tried AI. It was that Ford deployed it broadly and assumed it worked, rather than proving it on the job first.

**Why it matters to your team.** Read this as a case for testing, not as a case against AI. There are real tasks AI can take over, including ones that look like judgment, but you only learn which ones by testing and verifying in a safe way before you commit to them. Rolling a model out to everyone and hoping is the failure mode here, not the technology. Pick one job you are tempted to automate, run the AI version alongside the current process on real work, and judge it on a defect or quality signal you already track. Keep your experienced people in the loop while you learn, and let the evidence, not the hype, tell you where to hand over. Where AI does pass that bar, let automated checks and evaluations carry the quality at speed, rather than turning a person into a permanent bottleneck on every output.

**The human angle.** "AI is replacing you" and "AI failed, so humans win" are both the wrong message to send your team. Some old skills are becoming chalk-on-slate, genuinely obsolete and fine to lose, while the new load-bearing skill is the judgment to tell where AI earns its place and where it does not. No one yet knows where that line sits, so we test carefully and learn together, and the people with deep experience are exactly who we need both to judge the results and to grow that judgment in others. That framing protects morale and gets you better decisions.

### Item 3: Anthropic accuses a rival of lifting Claude's capabilities

**What happened.** Anthropic [accused Alibaba of illicitly extracting Claude's model capabilities](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/anthropic-says-alibaba-illicitly-extracted-claude-ai-model-capabilities-2026-06-24/), the highest-profile model-ownership dispute of this cycle. The claim is that a rival lifted what a frontier model can do, rather than building it independently.

**What shifted.** The signal is not the lawsuit itself, it is the assumption underneath it: that what a model can do is now defensible property worth fighting over, not just a product you license. That connects straight back to Item 1, because capability leaking across borders despite export controls is part of why governments are tightening the permission regime.

**Why it matters to your team.** You cannot audit a vendor's training data, so do not build your due diligence around pretending to. The operational move is contractual: ask whether the vendor will indemnify you if its model is found to infringe, and whether the model is subject to any pending infringement or extraction claim. Those are questions a procurement conversation can answer. A model caught up in an intellectual-property (IP) dispute carries the same kind of continuity risk as one under an export ban: it can be restricted, withdrawn, or forced to change while you are mid-project.

**The human angle.** This is a watch item, not a fire drill, and it is worth saying so plainly. The team does not need to react today. They need to know that "whose model is this, really" is now a question on the procurement checklist, so nobody is surprised when a vendor dispute lands in the news.

### Also worth noting

The accountability question got its first real test in late May, when a regional court in Munich issued a [preliminary injunction treating Google's AI Overviews as Google's own words](https://the-decoder.com/landmark-german-ruling-declares-googles-ai-overviews-are-googles-own-words-and-makes-it-liable-for-false-answers/) and barred it from spreading their false claims. This is not settled law: a lower court, an order that is not final, and Google is appealing. But it is the first ruling to say a deployer owns what its AI asserts, and if it holds, the exposure reaches any team running a customer-facing bot, not just Google. [Simon Willison's note](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/25/ai-and-liability/) is a clear read on why; worth a look now at where your own user-facing AI outputs sit and what they can assert.

Separately, [a sharp pullback in AI-exposed stocks](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/ai-rout-exposes-wall-street-s-270-billion-speculation-machine) this week is a reminder that a vendor's financial instability is one more way the model you depend on can be pulled out from under a project. That is three checks this issue puts on the procurement list: continuity, provenance, and financial stability. If you do only one this quarter, make it continuity: a tested fallback you control pays off whichever way the other two break.
