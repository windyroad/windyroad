# Issue 11: Frontier AI is now a permission, not a purchase

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-06-28*

From Tom: Ford spent this year quietly rehiring the veteran inspectors it had handed over to AI. I have a lot of sympathy and one strong opinion. The mistake was never trying AI on the job. The mistake was putting it in place for everyone and then hoping for the best. That is lazy, it is high risk, and honestly it is poor leadership. The truth is that no one really knows yet which jobs AI does well and which it quietly ruins, and in what situations. So the only responsible move is to test and learn, in a safe way, before you bet a production line or a release on it. That is the thread running through this week. The model itself is the easy part. The judgment about where to trust it, and whether you are even allowed to run it, is the part you cannot buy.

Last week we argued the model is the easy part. This week showed what the hard part is: control of the model is no longer something you buy along with it. Three stories, one pattern. Permission to run a model, proof it works for your job, and ownership of what it can do all became open questions in the same seven days.

### Item 1: Frontier model access is now a government permission

**What happened.** This week the US government showed it can both grant and withhold access to a frontier model, and used both powers at once. It eased the foreign-access ban on Anthropic, [clearing Claude Mythos 5 for "trusted" US organisations](https://www.semafor.com/article/06/27/2026/us-releases-powerful-anthropic-model-mythos-to-some-us-companies) as the two sides moved toward a deal. In the same days OpenAI [released only a limited preview of its next model, GPT-5.6 Sol](https://openai.com/index/previewing-gpt-5-6-sol/), [holding back the full launch while the government took early access](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/openai-defers-public-rollout-gpt56-us-seeks-early-access-frontier-ai-models-2026-06-26/) for a security review. A US lawmaker also [introduced a bill](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/us-lawmaker-proposes-bill-require-ai-companies-report-critical-incidents-2026-06-25/) that would require AI companies to report critical incidents.

**What shifted.** Two weeks ago this was a single off-switch. Last week it was a negotiation. This week it became a working permission system. The same government granted access to one vendor and restricted it for another within days, which hardens the control point from a one-off act into a repeatable, two-way process. Model access is no longer a property of the model. It is a permission, and a permission can be revoked as easily as it was granted.

**Why it matters to your team.** Treat model availability the way you already treat any single supplier that a regulator can switch off. Add a continuity clause to new and renewing AI-vendor contracts, with a stated notice period and an exit term for a model becoming unavailable. Keep one capable open-weight model that you can run yourself in your evaluation set, tested against a real workload, so a directive against your primary vendor does not strand a project mid-flight. The clause buys you notice. The tested fallback buys you somewhere to go. Neither is a memo for next quarter any more, because the trigger is already live.

**The human angle.** The people who carry this are whoever owns your vendor relationships, and they cannot make the uncertainty disappear. Giving them a quiet afternoon now to name and rehearse a fallback is far kinder than handing them a bad week when access changes without warning.

### Item 2: Ford rehired the inspectors it had given to AI

**What happened.** Ford has been [rehiring experienced quality inspectors after its AI inspection systems fell short](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/ford-has-been-rehiring-quality-inspectors-after-ai-fell-short) on the line. The experienced people it had moved aside were brought back when the automated systems did not hold up.

**What shifted.** This is the clearest production example yet of a lesson that keeps recurring in different forms: AI failed to replace a capability that proved harder to substitute than it looked. One company's reversal does not change how routine that underlying skill is across the industry, but it does sharpen the lesson. The expensive part was not that Ford tried AI. It was that Ford deployed it broadly and assumed it worked, rather than proving it on the job first.

**Why it matters to your team.** Read this as a case for testing, not as a case against AI. There are real tasks AI can take over, including ones that look like judgment, but you only learn which ones by testing and verifying in a safe way before you commit to them. Rolling a model out to everyone and hoping is the failure mode here, not the technology. Pick one job you are tempted to automate, run the AI version alongside the current process on real work, and judge it on a defect or quality signal you already track. Keep your experienced people in the loop while you learn, and let the evidence, not the hype, tell you where to hand over. Where AI does pass that bar, let automated checks and evaluations carry the quality at speed, rather than turning a person into a permanent bottleneck on every output.

**The human angle.** "AI is replacing you" and "AI failed, so humans win" are both the wrong message to send your team. The honest one is that no one yet knows where AI fits, so we are going to test carefully and learn together, and the people with deep experience are exactly who we need to judge the results. That framing protects morale and gets you better decisions.

### Item 3: A model's capabilities are now contested in court

**What happened.** Anthropic [accused Alibaba of illicitly extracting Claude's model capabilities](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/anthropic-says-alibaba-illicitly-extracted-claude-ai-model-capabilities-2026-06-24/), the highest-profile model-ownership dispute of this cycle. The claim is that a rival lifted what a frontier model can do, rather than building it independently.

**What shifted.** This is colour on the ownership question rather than a structural shift in the model market, but it connects straight back to Item 1. Capability leaking across borders despite export controls is part of why governments are tightening the permission regime in the first place. A model is no longer just a product you license. What it can do is now contestable property, and that contest can reach the vendor you depend on.

**Why it matters to your team.** Add model provenance to your vendor due diligence, alongside the financial-stability checks you already run. A model caught up in an extraction or intellectual-property (IP) dispute carries the same kind of continuity risk as one under an export ban: it can be restricted, withdrawn, or forced to change while you are mid-project. You do not need to follow the legal detail. You do need to know whether a model in your stack is under a cloud, and to keep the same tested fallback from Item 1 ready in case the answer changes.

**The human angle.** This is a watch item, not a fire drill, and it is worth saying so plainly. The team does not need to react today. They need to know that "whose model is this, really" is now a question on the procurement checklist, so nobody is surprised when a vendor dispute lands in the news.

### Also worth noting

The accountability question moved in the same direction off the back of an earlier-June European court ruling, which found Google liable for false answers its AI produced, treated as the company's own words. Simon Willison's [note on AI and liability](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/25/ai-and-liability/) is a clear read on why this matters: if a deployer owns the output, then any team shipping an AI feature whose answers reach users is closer to that liability than it was a month ago. It is worth a look at where your own user-facing AI outputs sit. Separately, a market wobble this week, [a sharp pullback in AI-exposed stocks](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/ai-rout-exposes-wall-street-s-270-billion-speculation-machine), is a reminder to keep vendor-financial-stability on the same procurement checklist as the continuity and provenance questions above.
