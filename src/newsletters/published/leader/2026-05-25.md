# Issue 06: AI's real prize is capacity, not a smaller team

*The Shift: AI engineering, week ending 2026-05-24*

**From Tom**

We are too early in this cycle for anyone to credibly promise that AI will cut your headcount, and anyone who does is overconfident. Every business process has a bottleneck, the one step that sets the pace for everything else. A process only gets faster if you speed up that step. But AI tends to get pointed at whatever is easiest to automate, which is rarely the bottleneck. Improve any other step and the process runs at exactly the same speed, so the efficiency that the headcount case depends on never arrives. Even when AI does hit the bottleneck, the constraint does not vanish; it moves to the next slowest step, and the real work becomes finding and fixing that one. That is a test-and-learn job, not a one-off saving. The bigger prize is usually more capacity from the same team than the same output from fewer people.

The same pattern runs through this week: AI looks like a cheap win at one step, but the real constraint, and the real cost, sit elsewhere. Three stories turn on it: the headcount case, the slop problem, and the patching gap. Shorter notes follow on agent-platform lock-in, the compliance clock, and what happens when you put aligned agents in a team.

### Item 1: Companies are cutting headcount for AI. The case got shakier

**What happened:**
  - May 22: Fortune reported that AI is proving more expensive to run than the staff it was meant to replace, with Nvidia's Bryan Catanzaro saying his team's compute now costs far more than its people, and Uber burning through its entire 2026 AI budget in four months ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/05/22/microsoft-ai-cost-problem-tokens-agents/)).
  - May 20: Intuit announced more than 3,000 layoffs to refocus on AI ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/20/intuit-to-lay-off-over-3000-employees-to-refocus-on-ai/)).
  - May 23: HSBC's chief urged its 211,000 staff to embrace AI rather than fight it, as the bank began cuts ([Yahoo Finance](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/hsbc-ceo-georges-elhedery-warns-141646153.html)).
  - May 23: Standard Chartered's CEO apologised for calling AI-replaceable staff "lower-value human capital" as it cuts about 8,000 roles, with regulators now asking questions ([SCMP](https://www.scmp.com/business/banking-finance/article/3354558/i-am-sorry-standard-chartered-ceo-bill-winters-apologises-comments-over-job-cuts)).

**Why it matters to your team:** a headcount-savings business case assumes the AI efficiency is both real and permanent. It is usually neither: real only if the AI lands on the bottleneck, permanent only if a new bottleneck does not take its place. A counter-narrative is already gaining ground that the companies cutting headcount for AI will lose to the ones that keep it ([Libertas](https://libertas.software/en/knowledge-hub/19/the-companies-cutting-headcount-for-ai-will-lose-to-the-ones-who-didnt)). Picture a mortgage lender whose growth is capped by how fast it can underwrite and process applications. Use AI there to cut headcount and you bank a small saving while the loan ceiling stays exactly where it was. Use it to raise capacity and the same team clears more applications, which lifts the ceiling on the whole business.

**The human angle:** the same choice is hitting software teams now, as AI makes engineering faster and the pressure builds to bank that as a smaller team. For most companies, what the business can ship and launch runs through engineering's delivery capacity, so the AI gain is worth more spent raising that capacity than cutting it.

**Try this:** before approving any AI initiative justified by headcount savings, ask for two numbers. First, the all-in cost of running the AI (tools, compute, and the people who oversee it), not just the salary it removes. Second, the extra throughput it delivers. If the throughput does not move, the AI missed the bottleneck and the saving is a guess. Book the saving only when that throughput shows up, never on the forecast. Banking gains before they arrive is how plans grow [McKinsey's "hairy back"](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/measuring-your-strategys-odds-of-success), and a headcount cut made on a gain that never lands leaves you short the people as well as the throughput.

### Item 2: More review will not fix AI slop

**What happened:**
  - May 22: the Wall Street Journal reported two of the engineers behind the agentic-coding boom warning of a "vibe slop crisis", where AI pumps out bloated, unrefactored code that hardens into permanent technical debt ([WSJ, via MSN](https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/technology/the-ai-superstars-who-say-a-vibe-slop-crisis-is-coming/ar-AA23Og8J)).
  - A widely-shared r/MachineLearning post echoed the mood, describing AI output pulling developers away from the work they care about ([r/MachineLearning](https://www.reddit.com/r/MachineLearning/comments/1tfv0vh/slop_is_making_me_feel_disconnected_from_ai/)).

**Why it matters to your team:** left alone, an AI-generated codebase drifts toward slop, the way any system drifts toward disorder when no energy goes into it. Physicists call that entropy, and the only way to reverse it is to keep putting energy back in. The instinct is to make that energy human review. At the volume AI produces, that fails two ways: review slides into a rubber stamp because no one can read that much generated code, and gating every change behind a person throttles AI back to human-reading pace. That second failure echoes Britain's early motor-car laws, which required someone to walk ahead of every vehicle waving a red flag. That flag held the car to walking pace and threw away the reason anyone bought one.

**The human angle:** the energy that reverses the decay has to scale with the generation, which means it belongs in the pipeline, not in a person reading diffs. Automated tests, architecture and convention checks, and agents and hooks that act on every change are what hold the line, and they keep AI's speed instead of cancelling it. Humans then spend their judgement on the few things only humans can judge, not on rubber-stamping every generated line.

**Try this:** build the counter-pressure into the pipeline first: convention and architecture checks that run on every change, a scheduled refactoring pass, and agents that enforce your standards automatically. Pull human review too early, before that automation has earned its place, and you just ship slop faster. So keep review running until the machinery holds the line on its own, then ease it back to the few calls only people should make.

### Item 3: AI now finds vulnerabilities faster than anyone can patch them

**What happened:**
  - May 22: Anthropic's Project Glasswing reported that around 50 partners (Cloudflare, Microsoft, Oracle, Palo Alto Networks, Mozilla, the UK AI Safety Institute) running its Claude "Mythos" preview against critical software found more than 10,000 high or critical vulnerabilities in a month ([Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/research/glasswing-initial-update)).
  - Mozilla alone found 271 Firefox vulnerabilities, over ten times its previous baseline, and the tool surfaced a wolfSSL flaw that allowed certificate forgery.
  - Of 530 bugs disclosed to maintainers, 75 are patched, and a critical bug now takes about two weeks to patch.

**Why it matters to your team:** on your security posture, the gain and the bottleneck are not the same step. AI sped up the step it is good at, finding bugs, by an order of magnitude, but the step that protects you, patching, did not move. The same flood of newly-public vulnerabilities is landing in the open-source dependencies you ship on, while your patch cycle stays where it was and attackers run the same discovery tools.

**The human angle:** this lands on your security team as a backlog that working harder will not clear, because detection now outruns any human queue behind it. The decision is no longer whether to hire more reviewers, it is how to triage and ship fixes faster.

**Try this:** measure your patch cycle for critical dependency vulnerabilities in days, and set a target for that number. Treat closing it as the security investment this quarter, because more scanning will not help if the fixes queue behind it.

### Also worth noting

**The labs are selling the platform now, not just the model.** DeepMind shipped [Gemini 3.5](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/gemini-models/gemini-3-5/), a model built to take actions, and [Antigravity 2.0](https://antigravity.google/blog/introducing-google-antigravity-2-0), a platform for building agents, while Anthropic [acquired Stainless](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-acquires-stainless), the software-development-kit plumbing that wires AI into your systems. It is easy to adopt and hard to leave, with no clear leader yet, so trial an agent platform on real production data and score switching cost before you commit.

**Stop waiting on US federal AI rules.** Washington drafted an AI executive order, then [pulled it](https://fortune.com/2026/05/22/tech-billionaires-convince-trump-to-back-off-ai-executive-order-but-much-of-maga-favors-ai-regulation/) under industry pressure. What binds you sits elsewhere, by reach not headquarters. The [EU AI Act's Article 50 transparency rules](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/transparency-rules-article-50/) apply (August 2026) if your AI reaches EU users; California's transparency laws apply if it reaches Californians, with new content-disclosure rules the same month. Inventory which customer-facing AI features trigger them before August.

**Aligning each agent does not align a team of them.** An [Anthropic-led study](https://alignment.anthropic.com/2026/ai-organizations/) found that across 12 business tasks, organisations of individually-aligned agents were more effective but less aligned than a single agent, sometimes doing unethical or illegal work none would do alone. The same week, the UK AI Safety Institute [asked](https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/will-it-become-harder-to-oversee-ai-systems) whether oversight gets harder as systems grow autonomous. Put your check on the team's output, not just each model.

**The memory shortage is now a line on your hardware budget.** AI data-centre demand is consuming memory production capacity and, [as Simon Willison reports](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/22/memory-shortage/), is already repricing consumer devices and components. If you have a hardware refresh or capacity expansion planned, the squeeze that is repricing phones is exposure on your bill of materials. Check your component quotes before you assume last year's pricing holds.
