# Issue 05: AI cyber capability went production this week. The counter-tools too

*The Shift: AI engineering, week ending 2026-05-15*

**From Tom**

Three stories below, one shape: capability, counter-tools, and the budget signals that decide which one your team gets. The cyber-capability shift from last week has a counter-tool story to match it this week, and the Amazon "tokenmaxxing" headline gets a more measured read than the headline suggests.

Plus shorter notes on the TanStack supply-chain incident, the Anthropic-Gates partnership, and OpenAI's ChatGPT-ads test.

### Item 1: AI cyber capability went production-real. Update your threat-model assumption

**What happened:**
  - May 7: OpenAI shipped GPT-5.5-Cyber and "Scaling Trusted Access for Cyber". This is a tier-1 lab releasing a frontier model in a security-specific vertical variant rather than as horizontal safety tooling. Last week's coverage named GPT-5.5 Bio Bug Bounty and OpenAI Privacy Filter; those were horizontal safety products. GPT-5.5-Cyber is a vertical product.
  - May 13: the UK AI Safety Institute (AISI) published ["How fast is autonomous AI cyber capability advancing?"](https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/how-fast-is-autonomous-ai-cyber-capability-advancing). The paper reports that autonomous task completion on cyber evaluations is "doubling every few months", with the acceleration outpacing prior trend predictions. That is a capability-acceleration measurement, not a point benchmark. It changes what evaluation frameworks have to account for.
  - May 11: [Google and the New York Times reported that criminal hackers used AI to develop a major software flaw](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/11/us/politics/google-hackers-attack-ai.html), a real-world AI-found exploit attributed to threat actors.

**Why it matters to your team:** *the attack toolkit and the counter-toolkit both shipped this week, on the same ledger.*

GPT-5.5-Cyber is the counter-tool. A top-tier vendor shipped a security-specific frontier model into limited preview the same week threat actors were credited with AI-found exploits.

If your team owns threat modelling, the review you scheduled in January is sized against the wrong capability curve. The vendor counter-toolkit now has a product that did not exist when you sized it. Three operational consequences follow:

  - **Threat-model cadence.** Enterprise reviews typically run annual or biannual; a doubling-every-few-months curve sits awkwardly against that. The review window is the lever to pull this quarter.
  - **Vendor selection.** AI-assisted security work now has a tier-1 product that did not exist last week. Procurement should evaluate it before the next renewal cycle.
  - **Benchmark currency.** The AISI capability-acceleration framing means a benchmark from six months ago is already out of date. Challenge any vendor pitch that relies on historical evaluation numbers.

**The human angle:** security work tends to compete with feature work for engineering time. The compounding-capability story moves the cost of deferring. Budgets sized at the start of this year were sized against last year's capability curve. The review window is the lever to reset that. Whoever owns the threat-model review owns that lever. The conversation with risk and compliance is therefore a calendar conversation about whose quarterly review takes priority.

**Try this:**
  1. Schedule a threat-model review inside the next four weeks, with the AISI doubling framing as the starting reference.
  2. Ask procurement to evaluate vendor-shipped AI security products (the GPT-5.5-Cyber tier) against in-house red-team budget for the next quarter.
  3. Shorten the threat-model review cadence from annual to quarterly while the capability-doubling curve persists.

### Item 2: Procurement just became a services-arm decision

**What happened:**
  - May 4 (last week's edition, recapped for context): Anthropic announced an enterprise AI services joint venture with Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman, and Goldman Sachs.
  - May 11: OpenAI launched its Deployment Company "to help businesses build around intelligence", a productized deployment-services arm. This is the second tier-1 lab in two consecutive weeks to productize a services arm.
  - May 13: Anthropic launched Claude for Small Business, a vertical offering that widens the same vendor surface to smaller buyers.

**Why it matters to your team:** *the AI procurement question is no longer which model, but which vendor's services arm.*

If you bought a model last year, your next AI procurement decision is about which vendor's services arm to engage, not which model to license. Two tier-1 labs in two weeks have shipped a services arm. Add a "who does the work" column to your AI-vendor evaluation, next to model and price. A 12-month services-arm deployment shapes what your team builds and buys. It also moves where the build-vs-buy line lands at the next annual budget. The Anthropic Claude for Small Business launch tells you the same pattern reaches teams below enterprise scale.

**The human angle:** a vendor services arm is a partner you co-staff with. Bringing one in puts engineering decisions into a shared room with the vendor, which means the vendor team sees your roadmap before your board does. Your engineers read the scope decision before they read the contract. If the scope cuts the work they were hired for, attrition starts in week two. Decide what stays in-house before you open the conversation, not after the statement of work is signed.

**Try this:**
  1. Add a "who does the work" column to the AI-vendor evaluation: model, price, contract, services arm.
  2. Before opening any vendor-services conversation, decide which workflows must stay in-house regardless of vendor offering.
  3. For any vendor-services proposal: label each workflow industry-standard (vendor product is fine) or business-differentiating (must stay in-house). With two vendors offering parallel services arms, the labelling exercise is now comparative.

**Source:**

### Item 3: Token-usage leaderboards are a kludge for AI experimentation. Better levers exist

**What happened:**
  - May 12: Ars Technica reported that Amazon employees are "tokenmaxxing", maximising AI-usage metrics under pressure to use AI tools. Our read is more measured than the headline: outright gaming is probably limited; the broader pattern is organisations using token volume as a proxy to encourage AI experimentation.
  - The same week, Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34 named "Agent instruction bloat" as a Caution this quarter: the engineering-practice version of the same failure mode, where instructions, notes, and context accumulate without pruning. A token-volume leaderboard rewards that accumulation instead of suppressing it. Radar also flagged "Codebase cognitive debt" as Assess on the adjacent risk class.

**Why it matters to your team:** *the experimentation intent is right; the volume-leaderboard mechanism is wrong.*

If your CTO sends you the Ars Technica piece this week, the answer is not "we'll watch for gaming". It is "we'll replace the proxy".

The underlying intent (encouraging AI experimentation) is genuinely valuable. Teams that run real experiments on AI tools learn faster than teams that wait for vendor consensus, and the learning compounds across quarters. The problem is the proxy.

A token-volume leaderboard rewards volume, not learning, and two failure modes follow. First, the harnesses we maintain in our own work show the pattern: skills, agents, and hooks tend to use more tokens as they evolve. Without active pruning, that drift compounds. A token-volume key performance indicator (KPI) removes the downward pressure that would otherwise check it. Second, the team that ships outcomes looks worse on the metric than the team that maximises wrappers around model calls. The work the metric was meant to surface goes unrewarded.

The constructive answer is to keep the experimentation intent and change the mechanism. Two concrete moves. First, name AI experimentation as an explicit workstream with documented learning outcomes. Second, stand up an internal open-source pattern for AI plugins. A plugin is a grouping of skills, agents, and hooks that other teams can adopt. Measure plugin adoption across teams. Reward the engineers whose plugins get picked up. That signal tracks the thing the volume leaderboard was a workaround for: whether one team's experimentation produces something the rest of the org wants to use.

**The human angle:** when a metric becomes a target, it stops being a useful measure. Token volume as an AI-usage proxy is a textbook case. Your engineers will follow your KPI signal whether or not it tracks what you want. If your signal says "use more tokens", the practice that emerges on your team will use more tokens. If your signal says "ship learning notes from AI experiments", the practice that emerges will ship learning notes. The structural fix is to replace volume-only metrics with paired outcome signals: shipped change, reviewer-acceptance rate, documented learning from a named experiment.

**Try this:**
  1. Name AI experimentation as an explicit workstream with its own time budget (one engineer-day per fortnight is a viable starting point) and a documented learning-outcome surface.
  2. Stand up an internal-OSS pattern for AI plugins (groupings of skills, agents, hooks that other teams can adopt). Measure plugin adoption across teams; reward the engineers whose plugins get picked up. That signal tracks what the volume leaderboard was reaching for.
  3. Build downward-pressure into anything that records information:
      - Schedule a regular pruning pass on AGENTS.md (or your equivalent agent-instructions file). It grows as people add notes without anything pruning the stale ones; any plugin that records information will bloat the same way.
      - Check prompt-injection hooks (UserPromptSubmit or equivalent) for per-turn re-injection that could be per-session.

### Also worth noting

- **OpenAI's TanStack npm supply-chain incident response (May 13).** A tier-1 lab walking through its response to a real-world supply-chain attack on AI-tooling dependencies. Reinforces this week's "the threat is no longer hypothetical" framing: vendor-shipped counter-tools meet adversarial supply-chain pressure on the same surface. If your team operates an npm-dependent pipeline (most JavaScript and AI-tooling stacks), this is a worked example to bring to the next security review. Source: [OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/our-response-to-the-tanstack-npm-supply-chain-attack/).

- **Anthropic and the Gates Foundation announced a $200M partnership (May 14).** If your team sells into public sector or philanthropy, the services-arm pattern just gained a reference customer. The Anthropic-Blackstone-Hellman & Friedman-Goldman JV and OpenAI's Deployment Company landed the pattern in enterprise; this lands it in public-sector procurement. Source: [Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/news/gates-foundation-partnership).

- **"The next phase of AI cybersecurity still needs humans" (Axios, May 14).** Counter-evidence on the AI-cyber-capability story above: even with vendor-shipped counter-tools and rising autonomous capability, human expertise remains load-bearing in cyber defence. Useful constraint for any procurement decision that assumes the new vendor cyber product replaces head-count. Source: [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/14/mythos-cyberscurity-human-ai-models).

- **OpenAI started testing ads in ChatGPT.** New contractual surface: where ads appear, what data trains them, who can opt out. If your team pastes internal data into ChatGPT, pull the enterprise contract and check the ad-targeting and training-data terms before the change ships at scale. Source: [OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/testing-ads-in-chatgpt/).
