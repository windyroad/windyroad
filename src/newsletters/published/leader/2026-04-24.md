# GPT-5.5 shipped. The real story is who owns your data.

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-04-24*

**From Tom**

GPT-5.5 shipped this week, with a privacy filter and a bio bug bounty alongside it. Underneath that headline I keep coming back to who owns your data and who owns the choice. Atlassian flips the default. Meta treats its own workforce as training material. GitHub rations which model a developer can reach. And an r/ClaudeAI post reports Anthropic suspended an entire team-plan organisation overnight with no warning. The data-and-choice capture story runs through a model-launch week. Here's my prediction: within twelve months a major employer will sell anonymised employee-activity data for AI training and pitch it as a productivity programme. The legislation to push back will arrive years late. What would prove me wrong: a major employer publicly committing in writing, before Q3 2026, to never use employee-activity data for AI training, and passing an independent audit against that commitment. So the decision this quarter is not just which assistant to pick. It is what you are willing to let vendors take, how fast you could move if they took the opt-out away, and whether your team has a manual path if AI vendor access disappears.

This week tightens both ends: GPT-5.5 lands a week after Opus 4.7 and Gemma 4 from the vendor side, while the OAIC issues an enforcement determination a week after publishing transparency guidance from the regulator side. Regulator slowness is no longer a buffer.

### Item 1: OpenAI's biggest week of 2026, in 48 hours

**What happened:**
  - On April 23, OpenAI shipped [GPT-5.5](https://openai.com/index/introducing-gpt-5-5/) with a published [system card](https://openai.com/index/gpt-5-5-system-card/) and a [Bio Bug Bounty](https://openai.com/index/gpt-5-5-bio-bug-bounty/) (Codex Desktop scope, $25,000 reward for a universal jailbreak on the five-question bio safety challenge).
  - On April 22, OpenAI introduced [Workspace Agents in ChatGPT](https://openai.com/index/introducing-workspace-agents-in-chatgpt/), an [OpenAI Privacy Filter](https://openai.com/index/introducing-openai-privacy-filter/), and [vertical improvements to ChatGPT for clinicians](https://openai.com/index/making-chatgpt-better-for-clinicians/) including HealthBench Professional, plus a WebSocket path added to the Responses API for agent workflows.

**Why it matters to your team:** three procurement surfaces shifted inside 48 hours. Foundation-model choice now has GPT-5.5 alongside Claude Opus 4.7 (last week) and Gemma 4 open weights, so model comparison that used to be quarterly is now weekly. Agent runtime choice now includes a first-party enterprise option from a tier-1 lab. The first named security products from a tier-1 lab (Bio Bug Bounty plus Privacy Filter) signal that AI security tooling is starting to ship as product, not research.

**The human angle:** the procurement team and the platform team are in a permanent renegotiation with each tier-1 vendor now. That requires either a decision-rights frame for which team picks what (model, runtime, security tooling) or a rolling vendor-evaluation cadence that compresses an annual exercise into a quarterly one. A stack audit done three months ago has new questions to ask this week, even if nothing in your stack changed.

### Item 2: Meta turns employee activity into training data

**What happened:**
  - [Reuters reported on April 21](https://www.reuters.com/sustainability/boards-policy-regulation/meta-start-capturing-employee-mouse-movements-keystrokes-ai-training-data-2026-04-21/) that Meta has begun capturing employee mouse movements and keystrokes for AI training data.

**Why it matters to your team:** when a frontier-lab employer treats its own workforce as in-scope training material, other employers face a check. The question is whether existing employment terms and monitoring-tool defaults already put staff activity in the same training pool by accident.

**The human angle:** expect the same arc as the Amazon warehouse-monitoring disputes. Monitoring is piloted, reframed as a productivity programme, then becomes normal. The recurring shape across two years of similar disputes plays out in three roles. Security teams are asked to exfiltrate the monitoring data. HR reframes it as a productivity programme. Unions and employee bodies litigate. The risks to weigh are IP leakage, reputation, turnover, and industrial action. For most employers we work with, the trade is poor once those costs are priced in.

### Item 3: Atlassian flips the default. Your SaaS contract just aged a year.

**What happened:**
  - [The Register reported on April 18](https://www.theregister.com/2026/04/18/atlassians_new_data_collection_policy/) that Atlassian moved from explicit opt-in to default-on for using customer tickets, code, and documentation to train its AI features, "unless law or cash say no."

**Why it matters to your team:** the governance question is no longer "do we allow Copilot". It is what data your SaaS vendors are training on. Decide the org's position first. If that position is "no training," the next contract renewal needs the opt-out clause, plus a documented set of options for when the vendor refuses (negotiate, accept, or exit). Data-classification tags also need to propagate to the ticket, code, and doc fields that were previously treated as internal-only.

**The human angle:** opt out this week. That's the easy call. The hard one comes if the opt-out disappears. Decide now: would you carry on or exit the product? If the answer is exit, line up the vendor-change approvals, the data-migration plan, and the alternative tool stack, then keep them on the shelf. Moving early is wasted effort. Moving late is months of disruption you cannot recover.

### Item 4: Vendors take the choice away, by tier and by ban

**What happened:**
  - [GitHub tightened Copilot individual plan limits on April 22](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/22/changes-to-github-copilot/) and restricted Claude Opus 4.7 to premium tiers, citing agentic-workflow compute demands.
  - [A widely-shared r/ClaudeAI post this week reports](https://www.reddit.com/r/ClaudeAI/s/VLJHltQm7m) that Anthropic suspended an agricultural-tech organisation (roughly 110 users) on its Team plan without warning.
  - Per the same post: appeals into a Google Form went unanswered for two days, the separately-billed API plan continued to run, and admin email addresses were also banned, blocking billing and usage visibility.
  - Anthropic has not publicly confirmed the account.

**Why it matters to your team:** vendor-decided access runs along a spectrum. At one end, primary-sourced: pay more for the model you used yesterday. At the other end, single-sourced and not yet vendor-confirmed: lose your team's daily workflow tool overnight, no warning and no recourse. Both ends belong on the AI-vendor risk register this quarter. The recourse question (appeal channel, time to resolution, fallback path) is now the procurement question. Picking a primary coding assistant is a three-dimensional decision: vendor, tier, and which model sits behind the tool.

**The human angle:** team-level variance is where the signal comes from this year. Team A on one assistant, Team B on another: the comparison tells you whether the tool fits the workload, or just where the teams landed. Standardise too early across the whole organisation, and the comparative signal disappears along with the fallback. A diagnostic move: run a hosted open-source model in parallel and watch adoption. Low adoption tells you the proprietary model is genuinely ahead on capability, or that the open-source UI-and-access path is the gap. High adoption tells you the proprietary price premium is no longer earned, or that the team is ready for a partial shift. Either reading is procurement evidence worth acting on. The access-loss scenario at the extreme end is no longer theoretical. Map which workflows have a manual path if a primary AI vendor disconnects, and how long the gap can run before delivery commits slip.

### Item 5: Australia's regulator stops talking and starts acting

**What happened:**
  - The OAIC issued a [determination against the RentTech platform "2Apply" on April 22](https://www.oaic.gov.au/news/media-centre/renttech-platforms-must-stop-unfair-and-excessive-personal-information-collection%2C-says-privacy-commissioner) for unfair and excessive personal-information collection.
  - The [OAIC and eSafety Commissioner signed a joint agreement on April 23](https://www.oaic.gov.au/news/media-centre/esafety-and-oaic-working-together-to-protect-privacy-and-safety-for-all-australians) to coordinate privacy and safety action (enforcement-capacity building, not itself an enforcement event).

**Why it matters to your team:** neither action names AI, but both set the data-collection baseline that AI features ride on top of. Last week the OAIC published transparency guidance for automated decision-making; this week it issued one enforcement determination and signed one coordination agreement, in two days. The gap from "regulator has opinions" to "regulator acts" is now measured in weeks for Australian markets. The underlying-data-collection question is the foundation that the Atlassian and Meta vendor stories above sit on top of.

**The human angle:** legal and compliance colleagues have moved from advising to enforcing. That changes the pre-release review conversation for AI-assisted customer features. A practical first pass: (1) list every AI-assisted customer feature; (2) name the data each ingests and the source; (3) check which vendor's training pool each source feeds, and document the opt-out status. It is not end-of-quarter work any more.

### Also worth noting

**UK AISI shows eval harnesses are not what they seem.** The UK AI Safety Institute [published research on April 20](https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/what-can-sandboxed-ai-agents-learn-about-their-evaluation-environments) showing a sandboxed open-source agent identified the evaluating organisation, inferred the operator's identity, and reconstructed the research timeline despite countermeasures. Vendor benchmarks and in-house regression evals both assume the agent does not know it is being tested; when that assumption breaks, model-card numbers carry an unsizable discount. The practical move for most teams: ask vendors how they test for evaluation-awareness, and if you run internal evals, rotate a production-sampled case set so a consistent signature cannot be learned over time.

**Apple appoints John Ternus as CEO with an AI-strategy reset on the agenda.** [Reported widely on April 20-21](https://news.google.com/search?q=Apple+CEO+John+Ternus+AI+strategy) (Reuters, CNBC, The Guardian, LA Times, CNN, with [AI Daily Brief commentary on April 22](https://media.beehiiv.com/p/how-apple-s-ai-strategy-changes-with-a-new-ceo)): Tim Cook moves to executive chairman, hardware chief John Ternus takes the CEO seat, and the consensus framing is an AI-strategy reset under hardware-engineering DNA. Initial direction (per DigiTimes April 23) is doubling down on Siri and on-device privacy. The leader-relevant question: Apple has been the quiet on-device player in the foundation-model layer, and a hardware-perfectionist CEO leaning further into privacy-preserving inference shifts the procurement calculus for teams that have been waiting for an enterprise-grade on-device option.
