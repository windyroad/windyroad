# Discipline is your AI lever this quarter.

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-06-07*

## From Tom

Discipline is the lever this week. The case sharpens because Anthropic is publicly calling for AI restraint while expanding faster than any other provider, in the same week.

Last week I called for discipline. Discipline as cadence is the hard part: most engineering teams I work with set a weekly review cadence in a planning meeting, then lose it within a fortnight when an exec dashboard pressures the team the other way. Discipline is the deliberate cadence at which your team's processes upgrade, not bureaucracy that slows them down. Not the discipline of refusing AI work outright, but the discipline of upgrading at the cadence AI ships.

The bottleneck in your team's work has moved off the model. It is on procurement, narrative survival, the security pipeline, cost discipline, and skill formation. Each one fixed cheaply this quarter becomes expensive to unwind in six months. The five items below are what I would push on first.

### Item 1: Capital keeps concentrating in AI infrastructure

**What happened.** Apollo wrapped its $35 billion debt deal to buy Google chips for Anthropic ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-05/apollo-wraps-up-35-billion-debt-to-buy-ai-chips-for-anthropic), Jun 5). The deal was first reported at $36 billion in late May and finalised at the revised number this week. SpaceX landed a $30 billion deal to provide Google with AI computing power ([NYT](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/05/technology/spacex-google-deal.html), Jun 5; [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/spacex-signs-cloud-deal-with-google-2026-06-05/), Jun 5). [Meta is weighing a major equity raise to finance AI infrastructure](https://www.reuters.com/technology/meta-weighs-big-equity-raising-finance-ai-infrastructure-ft-reports-2026-06-05/) (Reuters reporting on FT, Jun 5). [Marvell joined the S&P 500 after the AI boom helped the chipmaker pass profitability tests](https://www.reuters.com/business/marvell-join-sp-500-after-ai-boom-helps-chipmaker-pass-profitability-test-2026-06-05/) (Reuters, Jun 5).

**What shifted.** The foundation model layer keeps standardising while capital concentrates further inside a small number of substrate providers. Last week's substrate-concentration risk now has fresh structured-debt evidence behind it. That evidence is what makes a vendor financial-health check the right procurement-process trigger.

**Why it matters to your team.** Contract terms ripple through more layers than they did a year ago. Procurement evaluation needs vendor financial stability sitting beside capability and pricing in your next AI vendor renewal. A working rule of thumb: if one model or chip provider takes more than 25% of your quarterly AI spend, add a vendor financial-health check to your next procurement review. Apollo's $35 billion debt collateralised against chip purchases for Anthropic is why this is no longer routine due diligence.

**The human angle.** Procurement and finance need a shared view of AI vendor exposure that did not exist a year ago. The one-line frame to give your CFO: AI capital is concentrating into fewer chip and model providers at trillion-dollar scale, so vendor financial stability now matters as much as price. Without that frame, the conversation defaults to cost alone.

### Item 2: Anthropic argues for AI restraint while scaling its commercial reach

**What happened.** Reuters reported [Anthropic urges AI labs to pause development, warns humans risk losing control](https://www.reuters.com/business/anthropic-says-ai-labs-need-coordinated-plan-halt-development-if-risks-rise-2026-06-04/) (Jun 4). NYT ran [Anthropic's Call for A.I. Nonproliferation](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/06/05/business/dealbook/anthropic-ai-nonproliferation.html) (Jun 5). Axios followed with [Anthropic warns AI could soon help build its own successors](https://www.axios.com/2026/06/04/anthropic-warns-ai-build-successors) (Jun 4). Anthropic itself published [When AI builds itself](https://www.anthropic.com/institute/recursive-self-improvement) on its Institute site, an update on recursive self-improvement research (surfaced via Hacker News). The same week, Anthropic took three other steps. It [filed an S-1](https://www.anthropic.com/news/confidential-draft-s1-sec) (the SEC's pre-IPO paperwork) on Jun 1. It [expanded Project Glasswing](https://www.anthropic.com/news/expanding-project-glasswing) to roughly 150 organisations across 15-plus countries on Jun 2. And it [launched the Services Track and Partner Hub of the Claude Partner Network](https://www.anthropic.com/news/services-track-partner-hub) on Jun 3.

**What shifted.** Glasswing's reach is the load-bearing signal this week: the next capability tier is one step closer to general availability, which narrows the deployment-readiness window your team has.

**Why it matters to your team.** Internal AI deployment narratives now need to survive a substrate provider that is publicly arguing the opposite. That gap reaches your team in concrete ways:

- An employee surfaces the Anthropic non-proliferation statement in a Slack thread.
- A board member reads the NYT piece and asks the CEO about it at the next quarterly.
- A procurement counter-party cites it during contract negotiation.

Any narrative that frames headcount savings or speed wins as the primary justification has to survive those moments. Reassess internal AI-deployment talking points before the next all-hands. Removing bottlenecks in security patching processes belongs in the same conversation, because deployment readiness, not capability, is now the binding constraint.

**The human angle.** Engineering leaders carrying internal AI narratives need air cover from the CEO and CFO. Brief them on the substrate vendor's public position so the team answers questions consistently.

### Item 3: Anthropic and OpenAI both ship new AI security tooling

**What happened.** Anthropic published [What we learned mapping a year's worth of AI-enabled cyber threats](https://www.anthropic.com/news/AI-enabled-cyber-threats-mitre-attack), mapping AI-enabled threats to MITRE ATT&CK (Jun 3). Anthropic also released [`defending-code-reference-harness`](https://github.com/anthropics/defending-code-reference-harness) on GitHub, an open-source framework for AI-driven vulnerability discovery (Jun 3, surfaced via Hacker News). OpenAI launched [Lockdown Mode](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/5/openai-help-lockdown-mode/) to restrict outbound network requests (via Simon Willison, Jun 5). OpenAI also shipped [Strengthening societal resilience with Rosalind Biodefense](https://openai.com/index/strengthening-societal-resilience-with-rosalind-biodefense/) (May 29).

**What shifted.** AI security tooling moves from optional pilot to expected baseline. Two consecutive weeks of fresh artefacts from both Anthropic and OpenAI make a wait-and-see position harder to defend.

**Why it matters to your team.** Three concrete this-week actions:

1. Compare your threat model against Anthropic's MITRE ATT&CK mapping of AI-enabled threats. ATT&CK is the standard adversary tactics-and-techniques catalogue your security team likely already uses.
2. Evaluate the open-source vulnerability-discovery harness against your current SAST (static analysis) and DAST (dynamic analysis) coverage.
3. Review whether your AI tools expose outbound network requests that Lockdown Mode-class controls would limit.

Wire at least one of the three into your SDLC (software delivery lifecycle) before the next release.

**The human angle.** Security leads carry this work. Make sure the security pipeline owner is in the AI tooling conversation so the threat model is in the room when capabilities and pricing are.

### Item 4: OpenAI pushes Codex to every role while Uber caps developer AI spend

**What happened.** OpenAI pitched [Codex for every role, tool, and workflow](https://openai.com/index/codex-for-every-role-tool-workflow/) (Jun 2). The same week, Uber capped per-developer AI tool spend at $1,500 per month (via Simon Willison, [Uber's $1,500/month AI limit is a useful signal for AI tool pricing](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/3/uber-caps-usage/), Jun 3). This is the [same Uber we noted earlier in the cycle](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/issue-06-ais-real-prize-capacity-smaller-team-tom-howard-maicd-uabmc/) for burning through its full 2026 AI budget in four months: the cap is the company's response to its own burn rate. [AI Daily Brief](https://aidailybrief.beehiiv.com/) ran "The AI Token Shortage Begins" (Jun 2) and "How Companies Are Becoming AI Token Efficient" (Jun 5).

**What shifted.** The team-capability picture holds, but the two-pulls tension sharpens. Vendors are pushing universal distribution. Enterprises are setting hard per-developer caps. Both pulls land on the same engineering-leader move: buy what your team will use, and tie spend to a shipped-outcome metric.

**Why it matters to your team.** Pair a per-developer AI tool budget cap with a shipped-outcome metric before next quarter's budget review forces the conversation. Uber's $1,500-per-month datapoint is the public reference number. Pick whichever shipped-outcome metric your team already reports on (PRs merged per engineer per week, feature ship cadence, or mean time to resolve incidents work well) and put it next to the cap in the same dashboard. The risk: you commit to replacing headcount with AI based on what the tools can do, not what they cost to run.

**The human angle.** Developers feel a cap before they feel a procurement decision. Communicate the cap as paired discipline (capability spend and outcome metric together) so it lands as discipline, not austerity.

### Item 5: How engineers learn under AI, and how teams take contributions

**What happened.** The Daily Californian reported [failing grades soaring in UC Berkeley CS classes as AI usage rises and math foundations decline](https://www.dailycal.org/news/campus/academics/failing-grades-soar-as-professors-see-greater-ai-usage-dwindling-math-skills-in-uc-berkeley/article_16fad0bf-02cb-4b8c-8d88-888ffd9f8608.html) (surfaced via Hacker News). Charity Majors framed the practitioner divide as ["AI enthusiasts are in a race against time, AI skeptics are in a race against entropy"](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/4/ai-enthusiasts-ai-skeptics/) (via Simon Willison, Jun 4). Andreas Kling announced [the Ladybird browser project will no longer accept public pull requests](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/5/andreas-kling/) because of AI-submission load (via Simon Willison, Jun 5).

**What shifted.** New evidence in two directions: measured skill-formation failure (Berkeley) and a second high-profile project (Ladybird) attempting an outright opt-out, following Zig's earlier stance.

**Why it matters to your team.** Two distinct claims sit here. First, opting out of AI contributions the way Ladybird did is structurally unstable for any project that takes external contributions. Build guardrails that triage AI pull requests at AI speed instead. PR review and dependency patching are where to start. Second, skill formation under AI is a separate failure mode that does not get solved by PR guardrails. Track the share of code-review comments on junior-engineer PRs that flag a conceptual misunderstanding rather than a style nit: that share is the leading indicator worth watching. The Berkeley signal is a leading indicator for the talent pipeline every engineering org draws from.

**The human angle.** Charity Majors' entropy framing is worth naming in your next team meeting on AI tools. It gives both AI-leaning and AI-skeptical practitioners a shared vocabulary for the tradeoff they are making.

A second federal-policy mechanism worth watching alongside that one is civil liability. Politico ran the framing this week that [pending lawsuits against AI vendors could become the field's "Big Tobacco moment"](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/06/06/the-lawsuits-that-could-give-ai-its-big-tobacco-moment-00951764) (Jun 6), the first mainstream-political outlet we have seen pick up the analogy. Product-liability has been a known risk surface for AI vendors for some time. The framing graduating into political reporting matters because it changes who is reading about it.

Both threads point to one place: your next AI vendor renewal review. Add a federal-acquisition-clause line item if you or your vendors sell to federal customers, and a duty-of-care clause review if the Big Tobacco framing changes your civil-liability exposure. Brief legal counsel on the duty-of-care framing while you are there.
