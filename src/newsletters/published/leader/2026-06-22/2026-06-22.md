# Issue 10: The hard part of AI is no longer the model

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-06-21*

From Tom: There is a story, probably made up, that a school once fretted children given paper and pencils would lose the ability to write on slate with chalk, without getting chalk all over themselves. True or not, that skill vanished and nobody missed it. As a teenager I could recite the phone number of every friend and family member. That skill is gone too, and I am fine. So when [the first hard data](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01947-1) arrives showing AI is eroding some skills, I do not reach for alarm. I ask a different question: which skills are becoming the chalk-on-slate of our trade, and which new ones do we now have to build and support?

That is a leader's job this week, and it is not the only thing that moved off the model. Three other constraints did too. A government is turning the off-switch on frontier models into an ongoing negotiation. The public has soured on the word "AI" itself. And the one technical move still worth your time is defensive: keep a top open-weight model, one you can download and run yourself, in your evaluation set. The throughline this week is that the model is the easy part.

### Item 1: The off-switch is becoming a government negotiation

**What happened.** Last week a US directive forced Anthropic to suspend foreign access to its two most capable models, Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5, a restriction that [a competitor's lobbying reportedly helped trigger](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/amazon-ceos-talks-with-u-s-officials-triggered-crackdown-on-anthropic-models-dcc90578) (Wall Street Journal, Jun 13). This week that one-off act turned into a negotiation. White House talks with Anthropic have [shifted from the single restriction to setting standing AI-security rules](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/06/18/white-house-talks-with-anthropic-shift-to-setting-ai-security-rules-00967758) (Politico, Jun 18). In the same days, President Trump said he [no longer views Anthropic as a national security threat](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/trump-tells-axios-he-no-longer-views-anthropic-national-security-threat-2026-06-19/) and called the talks positive (Reuters, Jun 19). The original restriction's [legality is also being questioned](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/06/18/trump-anthropic-ai-export-controls-00966118) (Politico, Jun 18). Meanwhile customers are not waiting for the outcome: JPMorgan [cut Anthropic's Claude access for its Hong Kong staff](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/jpmorgan-chase-cuts-off-anthropic-access-its-hong-kong-staff-ft-reports-2026-06-18/) (Reuters, Jun 18), and the trade press is already [tracking the models teams are moving to](https://aidailybrief.beehiiv.com/p/the-models-trying-to-replace-fable) (Jun 19).

**What shifted.** In last week's edition the off-switch was a single event. This week it became a negotiation. The President softened his public line in the same days the talks moved toward standing rules. That means a model's availability now rides on a government deal that can swing either way, week to week. That is a different kind of risk from a one-off act: it is ongoing, it sets a precedent other governments can copy, and the outcome is being bargained in public. The control point on the model supply chain is now political, not technical.

**Why it matters to your team.** Last week the continuity question was hypothetical. This week a frontier model's availability is openly riding on a political negotiation, which means a tested fallback is no longer a memo for next quarter. The softening does not remove the risk; it confirms the shape of it, because a deal that can ease can also tighten. Stand up an alternate model path now and run a real workload through it, so a directive, an export-control action, or a contested-but-in-force rule cannot strand a project mid-flight. Add vendor-continuity to your procurement criteria alongside vendor-financial-stability. The question is not only whether your vendor will still exist, but whether it will still be allowed to serve you.

**The human angle.** The people who carry this are whoever owns your vendor relationships, and they cannot make the uncertainty go away. Giving them a calm afternoon now to name and test a fallback is kinder than handing them a bad week when access changes without notice.

### Item 2: The public has turned on the word "AI"

**What happened.** Public sentiment turned negative in hard numbers this week. A Future of the Web survey found that [around 60% of US consumers say "AI" in brand messaging is a turnoff](https://wpvip.com/future-of-the-web-2026/). A separate study reported that [only 16% of Americans think AI will have a positive impact on society](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/17/only-16-percent-of-americans-think-ai-will-have-a-positive-impact-on-society-a-new-study-shows/) (Jun 17). In policy, Norway [imposed a near-ban on AI in elementary schools](https://www.reuters.com/technology/norway-imposes-near-ban-ai-elementary-school-2026-06-19/) (Jun 19).

**What shifted.** AI fatigue stopped being a vibe and became a measured constraint. For most of the past year the working assumption was that putting AI in front of users and "AI-powered" on the box was a tailwind. The data this week says it is often a headwind, and a second jurisdiction has now translated that mood into law for one setting.

**Why it matters to your team.** This is a comms and product-messaging question you can act on this quarter, not a culture-war one to argue about. Audit where your product and your internal change messaging lead with the "AI" label rather than the outcome, and test removing it. Run the labelled version against an unlabelled one and judge it on a signal you already track, like activation, conversion, or repeat use, not on whether people like the technology. The teams that win here sell the result and keep the AI quiet, rather than the reverse.

**The human angle.** Your own engineers and customers live in the same sentiment shift. An internal rollout that leads with "we are an AI-first company" can land as a threat where "here is a tool that removes a job you hate" lands as a gift. The framing is free to change and expensive to get wrong.

### Item 3: Skill erosion is now measured. Build the new skills, don't mourn the old

**What happened.** Some of the first hard data on AI and human skill arrived this week, and [the early results are not encouraging](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01947-1) (Nature). It landed the same week a respected engineering voice argued that [AI demands more engineering discipline, not less](https://charitydotwtf.substack.com/p/ai-demands-more-engineering-discipline), as code shifts from carefully curated to cheap and instantly regenerable.

**What shifted.** A skill-erosion worry we have tracked as campus anecdote now has a peer-reviewed measurement behind it. The study is early, just one finding, not a settled verdict. But moving from anecdote to measurement is a real upgrade in evidence, and worth taking seriously. The more useful leadership question, though, is not how to preserve every skill AI touches. Some of what AI erodes is chalk-on-slate, genuinely obsolete and fine to lose. Some of it is the opposite of obsolete: the judgment to set technical direction and catch a costly architecture mistake before it ships. That is the discipline worth protecting, and the discipline that piece argues for. The job is to tell the two apart.

**Why it matters to your team.** If you set training budget or run capability planning, treat this as a capability-investment decision, not a hand-wringing exercise. Name the skills that are becoming redundant and stop spending training budget defending them. The new load-bearing skills are not faster code review. They are architectural and product judgment: ratifying the technical and product-direction decisions that keep software aligned with the business and head off expensive mistakes. Those decisions sit off the critical path, so keep your people there. Let automated gates and evaluations carry code-level quality at AI pace, rather than making a human reviewer the bottleneck every change waits on. Measure defect-catch rate and delivery flow, not how much code your people personally reviewed.

**The human angle.** "AI is making you worse at your job" is a demoralising message and a half-true one. "Some of your old skills matter less now, and here are the new ones we are going to grow together" is both kinder and more accurate. Engineers can hear the second one and act on it.

### Item 4: A second open model just reached the frontier

**What happened.** Z.ai shipped GLM-5.2, which Simon Willison called [probably the most powerful text-only open-weights model available](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/17/glm-52/), ranking first among open models on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index at 753 billion parameters with a one-million-token context window (Jun 17). It is the second capable open-weight release in two weeks, after Google's Gemma 4, and the two sit at very different sizes: GLM-5.2 is a 753-billion-parameter heavyweight, Gemma 4 a far smaller 12-billion-parameter model.

**What shifted.** The open-weight frontier is now consistently close to the closed frontier on text, and capable open models are shipping fast enough that self-hosting is a live procurement choice rather than a research curiosity. The question is no longer whether an open model can do the work. It is which one fits the workload you need to protect.

**Why it matters to your team.** This is the concrete answer to Item 1. A top-ranked open-weight model you can host yourself is both a cost hedge and a sovereignty hedge: nobody can switch it off. Pull a capable open model into your evaluation set now: GLM-5.2 if you need frontier-grade text, Gemma 4 if you need something lighter to self-host. Benchmark it against your incumbent closed model on a real workload, so the fallback in Item 1 is tested before you need it rather than after. Because Item 1's off-switch is already live, this is a fallback to stand up now, not one to watch.

**The human angle.** An evaluation like this is satisfying work that engineers tend to enjoy. It pays off twice: a sharper view of what your incumbent is worth, and a continuity plan that lets the team sleep through the next off-switch headline.

### Also worth noting

OpenAI spent the week professionalising the commercial layer rather than the model, shipping [enterprise spend controls and usage analytics](https://openai.com/index/chatgpt-enterprise-spend-controls/) (Jun 18) and launching the [OpenAI Partner Network](https://openai.com/index/introducing-openai-partner-network/) (Jun 14), extending the same sell-services-alongside-the-model pattern Anthropic moved on earlier. When the largest model providers put their energy into budgets, partners, and governance rather than raw capability, that is the same signal as the rest of this issue from the vendor side. The model is no longer where the hard problems are.
