# The UK AI Safety Institute just confirmed a frontier model can run a full cyber-attack. Update your threat model

*The Shift: AI engineering, week ending 2026-05-08*

**From Tom**

This week the UK AI Safety Institute (AISI) confirmed GPT-5.5 can run a multi-step cyber-attack from start to finish. It is the second model to clear that bar. The first was Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview. AISI named the threshold, named the models that crossed it, and published the success rates. Attacker capability is now something you can measure, not something vendors claim.

Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34 landed alongside. Five AI-engineering practices moved to Adopt (the Radar's strongest "use this" rating). "Agent exfiltration risks" moved to Caution (the Radar's "proceed carefully" rating). Two trusted bodies, in the same week, named maturity tiers in adjacent parts of AI security: capability evaluation on one side, engineering practice on the other. The harder question this week is what your threat model assumes about attacker capability, and what your engineering practice assumes about agent risk. Four stories below, plus shorter notes on ChatGPT ads, compute capacity, and other items.

### Item 1: AISI verified a cyber-attack capability tier. Update your threat model to match

**What happened:**
  - April 30: the UK AI Safety Institute published its evaluation of OpenAI's GPT-5.5 cyber capabilities.
  - GPT-5.5 is one of two models AISI has evaluated as completing a multi-step cyber-attack simulation end-to-end. The first was Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview, which completed AISI's corporate network attack simulation end-to-end; GPT-5.5 followed.
  - AISI's published Expert-task scores: GPT-5.5 71.4% (plus or minus 8.0%), Mythos Preview 68.6% (plus or minus 8.7%). The intervals overlap; treat the two scores as comparable, not ranked. Specific challenges named in the post include "The Last Ones" (GPT-5.5 completed 2 of 10 attempts) and "rust_vm" (GPT-5.5 solved in 10 minutes 22 seconds at $1.73 USD).
  - Anthropic has held Mythos back from public release. The company organised a coalition it calls Project Glasswing, with Apple, Microsoft, JPMorgan Chase, and others, to patch the thousands of vulnerabilities Mythos has already unearthed across major operating systems and web browsers before any wider release. Pre-release multi-company vulnerability coordination at this scale is unusual; the participating coalition members have judged the model risky enough to warrant the delay. GPT-5.5, the second model AISI named, is already publicly available via the API.
  - May 4-5: the Trump White House reversed course on AI safety oversight in response to the AISI Mythos finding. NYT broke the story (May 4); the policy under consideration is pre-release vetting of frontier AI models, with Google, Microsoft, and xAI named as test subjects. Reporting points to a new US safety-testing body taking on the role that NIST's AI Safety Institute lost at the start of the current Trump term. Anthropic's CEO had visited the White House over Mythos hacking concerns the prior month (Washington Post, April 17).

**Why it matters to your team:** the threat is attackers using frontier models against your perimeter. AISI's evaluation reads as a defender's benchmark. A malicious actor with access to a current-tier model can run a full multi-step attack chain at the demonstrated success rate. One named challenge solved at $1.73. Three operational consequences. First, update your threat model to assume that success rate. Your last red-team exercise probably assumed a lower one. Second, ask your pen-test vendor to confirm recent engagements used current-tier models for the LLM-driven portions of the attack chain. Third, detection-and-response playbooks need to handle multi-step LLM-driven attack chains, not single-shot exploit attempts.

**The human angle:** threat-modelling work drifts to the bottom of the calendar. It is never urgent until it is. AISI's evaluation hands security review a calendar trigger this quarter. The team that runs that conversation now gets to shape the budget for the next round of red-team and detection spend. The team that defers will be working next year with a budget sized for last year's threat.

**Try this:**
  1. Add "what capability tier does our threat model assume" to the next security-review agenda.
  2. Confirm with your pen-test vendor that recent engagements used current-tier models for the LLM-driven portions of the attack chain.
  3. Audit detection-and-response playbooks against multi-step LLM-driven attack chains, not single-shot exploit attempts.
  4. Surface the AISI capability tier and the named threshold-crossing models to whoever owns threat modelling.

### Item 2: Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34: five AI-engineering practices in Adopt, agent exfiltration in Caution

**What happened:** Thoughtworks Radar Vol 34 published this edition cycle. Five AI-engineering practices moved to Adopt:

  - **Spec-driven development**: workflows guiding agents through planning, design, and implementation.
  - **Zero trust architecture**: least-privilege foundation for agent systems.
  - **DORA metrics** (DevOps Research and Assessment delivery measures): counterweight to AI-driven complexity.
  - **Mutation testing**: deterministic quality gate for agent feedback loops.
  - **Measuring collaboration quality with coding agents**: the Radar's framing is that it "redefines what it means to be a software developer in agentic systems".

The Radar's Trial ring sits one rung below Adopt. Trial entries this volume that signal the next adoption wave: Agent Skills, Feedback sensors for coding agents, Architecture drift reduction with LLMs, and Codebase cognitive debt. Trial-ringed tools include OpenClaw, GitHub Spec-Kit, and OpenSpec. Model Context Protocol moved into Trial on Platforms.

In the Caution column, the Radar called out **Agent exfiltration risks**: "models find creative paths to leak data without malicious intent or explicit prompting". This is the second leader-trusted body this week (alongside AISI in Item 1) naming a concrete AI security risk class to track.

**Why it matters to your team:** the Radar publishes once or twice a year. When five AI-engineering practices land at Adopt in a single volume, the practice has hit a maturity tier. Your CI pipeline now has a visible gap if it does not run mutation testing on agent-generated code. The same goes for any deployment that grants agents broader access than zero-trust principles allow. The Radar makes both gaps public. Risk-register implication: agent exfiltration belongs alongside the AISI capability tier from Item 1, but they sit on different surfaces. Agent exfiltration is your own deployed agents leaking data on the way out. The AISI tier is external attackers using frontier models against you on the way in.

**The human angle:** the Radar reading is what senior engineers and leaders cite to each other when neither has time to make the case from scratch. Teams that have run a quarterly self-assessment against the Adopt list arrive at planning conversations with a current scorecard. Teams without one rebuild the case each cycle.

**Try this:**
  1. Pull the team's current AI-engineering practice list (or build one if it does not exist).
  2. Score each Radar Adopt item: practiced, partially practiced, not yet practiced. The two most likely missing in AI-engineering work are mutation testing on agent-generated code and zero-trust on agent deployments; start there.
  3. Pick one not-yet-practiced item to land this quarter; pick one partially-practiced item to lift to fully practiced.
  4. Add agent-exfiltration as a row on the AI-vendor risk register, with the Radar's framing as the trigger description.

### Item 3: Google Chrome is loading a 4GB AI model on user devices and reinstalling it after deletion. Audit what's in your stack you didn't put there

**What happened:** Google Chrome is downloading a 4GB Gemini Nano AI model to user devices without explicit consent. PCWorld, Tom's Hardware, Decrypt, and gHacks all reported it this week. **The model reinstalls after deletion**, per multiple outlets: vendor software resisting user removal is the line that turns this from a configuration question into an IT-policy one. Researchers cited in the coverage flagged potential EU-law conflicts and material energy waste at fleet scale. No regulator has acted as of publication.

**Why it matters to your team:** Item 1's threat model is about external attackers using frontier models. This story is the inverse: a vendor is putting AI capability onto your endpoints that your team did not deploy and cannot audit. For a Chrome-managed fleet, "what AI is on our devices" is now a different question to "what AI did we deploy". Three operational consequences. First, endpoint inventory needs an AI-capability column, not just a software-version column. Second, IT policy on default-browser configuration is now an AI-governance decision, not just an end-user-experience decision. Third, your data-loss-prevention (DLP) tools can no longer see what the model does with local data. A 4GB local model can answer questions about local data without the data ever leaving the device.

**The human angle:** vendor-pushed AI on managed devices puts IT, security, and procurement in the same conversation: what is in our stack that we did not put there, and who is accountable when it leaks data the user-facing tier assumes is private. The conversation was inside the company three months ago. It has an external party in the room now.

**Try this:**
  1. Confirm whether your Chrome-managed fleet has Gemini Nano installed (the file is named `weights.bin`, roughly 4GB).
  2. Decide IT-policy posture: leave installed, block via Chrome enterprise policy, or audit-only.
  3. Add "vendor-installed AI capability on endpoints" as a new line on the AI-vendor risk register (the same register Item 2 added agent exfiltration to).
  4. Surface the IT-policy decision to security review, not just procurement.

### Item 4: Vendors are selling vertical agents now. Decide what is product-replaceable before you sign

**What happened:**
  - Anthropic announced a joint venture with Blackstone, Hellman & Friedman, and Goldman Sachs to form a new enterprise AI services company (May 4).
  - Anthropic shipped a financial-services agents product (May 5): ten ready-to-run agent templates available as plugins in Claude Cowork and Claude Code, plus cookbooks for Claude Managed Agents.
  - OpenAI announced a PwC partnership to "reimagine the office of the CFO" (May 5).
  - DeepMind published an AI co-clinician research collaboration (April 30). The article is explicit that the system is research-stage with academic and research collaborators, not for use in diagnosis, treatment, or medical advice. Cited here as the research-collaboration counterpoint to the productization track Anthropic and OpenAI took, not as a shipped vertical product.

**Why it matters to your team:** the build-vs-buy frame now has a third option (contract the vendor), and a vertical-product shelf to buy from. The deeper question is whether the workflow is product-replaceable or bespoke. If a workflow is industry-standard, the vendor's offer probably moves you faster than building from scratch. If the workflow is what differentiates your business, watch what the vendor is selling next. A vendor selling your edge to a competitor next quarter is a different kind of partner than a tools provider. Apply the test before you sign or contract; reapply it before you adopt the vertical product. Worked example from this week: the Anthropic finance-agents product is the clearest "shipped vertical product" case; the Anthropic-Goldman-Blackstone-H&F JV is the clearest "contract the vendor" case; the OpenAI-PwC partnership sits closer to "contract" than "product"; the DeepMind co-clinician is still research, not anything to procure yet.

**The human angle:** vendor-led services contracts move accountability for outcomes to a contract clause. That is fine for industry-standard workflows and a different shape of risk for the workflows that differentiate your business. Knowing which workflows fall on which side of that line is what keeps strategic control even when vendor velocity is attractive.

**Try this:**
  1. Pull the most recent vendor-services proposal or contract on the table.
  2. For each workflow it covers, label: industry-standard (vendor product is fine) or business-differentiating (must remain bespoke).
  3. If any business-differentiating workflow is included in the vendor scope, flag it for renegotiation before signing.
  4. Surface the labelled list to whoever owns procurement and to whoever owns the engineering relationship.
