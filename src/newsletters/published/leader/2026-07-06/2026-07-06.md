# Issue 12: The model flip-flopped this week. Your talent and payoff shouldn't.

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-07-05*

From Tom: This week a government switched a frontier model back on, under three weeks after switching it off. In practice it just delayed things. Once access was pulled you could not evaluate the model or plan around it, and no one knew when it would return, so anyone weighing Anthropic's Fable 5 was left waiting. I want to make a different point than "hedge your vendor", which is by now a recurring theme in The Shift. The model layer is going to keep lurching: a cheaper option one week, a new one held back for government review the next, and little you can do to speed either up. So don't let it set your pace.

The decisions that compound for your team reward a steady hand: turning junior engineers into the seniors you will need, and proving the AI spend turned into something you can bank. That first one is your bench, and every future senior comes off it. Neither decision flip-flops unless you flip-flop it.

This week it shows up in three: a public split over whether to keep hiring juniors, the biggest AI spenders starting to count the cost, and a frontier model switched off and then back on.

### The AWS CEO is hiring juniors on purpose. That is the tell.

**What happened.** The argument over whether AI kills the junior developer split into the open this week, and the biggest cloud provider came down hard on one side. AWS chief executive Matt Garman [explained why Amazon is hiring roughly 11,000 interns and junior employees](https://www.platformer.news/matt-garman-aws-ceo-interview-ai-jobs/) rather than thinning them, restating his blunt standing view that using AI to displace entry-level staff is one of the dumbest ideas he has heard. That is the same week the harsher take was doing the rounds, that [AI has torched the market for junior programmers](https://seldo.com/posts/ai-has-torched-the-market-for-junior-programmers/) altogether. Both can be true at once: the junior market can be under real pressure, as that harsher take argues, and hiring juniors anyway can still be the right long-term call.

**Why it matters to your team.** If you have slowed or paused junior hiring because AI now covers the simple work, treat this as the prompt to write down who becomes your senior engineers in five years. Cutting the bottom rung is not a cost saving, it is a bet that you will never need the people that rung produces. The AWS position is the one worth borrowing: juniors are how an organisation grows the judgment that AI cannot yet supply, so the real question is not "can AI do junior work" but "where do our next seniors come from if we stop growing any". Name that answer before the next budget review makes the decision for you by default.

**The human angle.** Your junior engineers read the same headlines you do, and "AI is coming for the entry level" is a frightening one to sit under. Telling them plainly that you are investing in them, and saying why, is both the kinder move and the one that keeps them long enough to become the seniors you will need. Silence reads as quiet agreement with the people predicting their jobs away.

### The AI money still isn't obviously coming back

**What happened.** The spending is real and the payoff is still hard to show. An MIT study last year found that [95% of organisations reported no measurable return on their generative-AI investment](https://www.forbes.com/sites/jasonsnyder/2025/08/26/mit-finds-95-of-genai-pilots-fail-because-companies-avoid-friction/), even as the money going in kept climbing. This week the counting reached the biggest spenders. Meta [moved to cap employee AI token usage](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/tokenminimizing-meta-moves-curb-employee-ai-usage-ai-costs-reach-billions) after the internal bill ran toward the billions, and SAP [restricted hiring and travel to fund its AI push](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-02/sap-restricts-hiring-travel-to-fund-significant-ai-push), narrowing new hiring to "core AI roles". Microsoft read the same gap as an opening and [launched a $2.5 billion company](https://www.reuters.com/business/retail-consumer/microsoft-launches-firm-help-companies-adopt-ai-with-25-billion-2026-07-02/) to embed its people inside customer businesses and help them pick AI that returns something. Capping spend is not proof that AI is not paying off, some of it plainly is. But when the largest spenders start counting the cost, the era of open-ended AI budgets is closing, and the next question every board asks is what came back.

**Why it matters to your team.** The gap here is not capability, it is capture. Usage tells you people are picking up the tools. It tells you nothing about whether the money came back. Change what you make AI initiatives prove: not seats filled, not tokens burned, not hours nominally saved, but a number you can bank, revenue earned, an outcome shipped, or a cost genuinely removed. If an initiative cannot name that number, it is a science project, and science projects are fine as long as you funded them as science projects rather than as returns.

**The human angle.** A team told to "use more AI" will show you usage, because usage is what you asked for. Ask for captured value instead and you change what they optimise for, away from looking busy with AI and toward proving it worked. That is the fairer measure too. It rewards the engineer who quietly removed a real cost over the one who ran up the biggest token bill.

### A government switched a frontier model off, then back on

**What happened.** Under three weeks after a US directive [suspended access to Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5](https://www.anthropic.com/news/fable-mythos-access) models, the government [lifted the restrictions](https://www.reuters.com/business/us-lift-export-controls-anthropics-fable-ai-model-tuesday-source-says-2026-06-30/) and Anthropic [redeployed Fable 5 worldwide](https://www.anthropic.com/news/redeploying-fable-5). Two quieter moves from the same fortnight matter more for planning than the reversal does. OpenAI [delayed the public launch of GPT-5.6 Sol at the government's request](https://www.reuters.com/legal/litigation/openai-defers-public-rollout-gpt56-us-seeks-early-access-frontier-ai-models-2026-06-26/), limiting early access to a small set of vetted partners. A new executive order gives officials up to thirty days with a frontier model before release. And Alibaba [moved to ban its staff from Anthropic's Claude Code](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/alibaba-ban-claude-code-workplace-over-alleged-backdoor-risks-source-says-2026-07-03/) over alleged surveillance risks, giving them until July 10 to switch to a domestic tool.

**What shifted.** We have said twice that a government can switch your model off. Fable is back and unlikely to flip off again, the export controls lifted and the review cleared, but the more useful update is quieter: your access to the model and tool layer keeps moving on decisions you do not make. Getting the next frontier model is now a licensing question with unfinished rules. Sol reached the public through a government review rather than a plain launch, so a vendor's ship date is no longer the date you get the model. Alibaba banning Claude Code is the same lesson from another direction: a tool a team relies on can be pulled over trust or security concerns with only days of notice. Those timelines are not yours to plan around.

**Why it matters to your team.** Do not put an unreleased model on your critical path. If a plan only works once you have the next frontier model by a certain quarter, you have borrowed against a timeline a government now partly controls, and no one will tell you when it slips. Be patient with the model you have been promised, and build the parts of the roadmap that matter on what you can run today. A capable open-weight model you host yourself is the part of this you fully control. It trails the frontier, so scope the work that cannot stall to what an open model can do today. The notes below show those options maturing. Treat the next frontier model as an upgrade you take when it clears, not a foundation you pour before it arrives.

**The human angle.** The people who own your vendor roadmap are the ones who feel this, because they are the ones asked to promise a date. Give them permission to plan for the model in your hands rather than the one on the vendor's slide, and to push back when a roadmap quietly assumes an unreleased model lands on time. Patience here is not passivity. It is refusing to make a commitment on someone else's clearance timeline.

### Also worth noting

Two smaller threads round out the week, and both sit on ground you control. First, the self-hostable fallback from the third story keeps maturing. The open-weight frontier is where that happens: models whose weights are published, so you can download and run them yourself. The community keeps filling the gaps vendors leave, most recently [extending Google's Gemma 4 to a larger 44-billion-parameter build](https://www.reddit.com/r/LocalLLaMA/comments/1ul0cx9/i_extended_gemma431b_to_44b_88_layers_since/) because Google had not shipped one that big. Simon Willison also surfaced [an Open Source AI Gap Map](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/3/open-source-ai-gap-map/), a catalogue of 421 open projects that tracks where open source still trails, so you can see what is covered before you commit. Second, the Godot game engine [will no longer accept AI-authored code contributions](https://www.pcgamer.com/gaming-industry/open-source-game-engine-godot-will-no-longer-accept-ai-authored-code-contributions-we-cant-trust-heavy-users-of-ai-to-understand-their-code-enough-to-fix-it/), saying it cannot trust heavy AI users to understand their own code well enough to fix it. That makes Godot the third notable open-source project we have tracked taking this stance, after Zig and Ladybird. We have argued before that a blanket ban is the unstable answer: the contributions keep coming, and the durable move is to build gates that triage AI-written code at machine speed rather than refuse it. Godot is your prompt to write that policy deliberately, before a reflex or an auditor writes it for you.
