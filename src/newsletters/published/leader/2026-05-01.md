# Six AI shifts this week. All of them measurement problems

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-05-01*

**From Tom**

AI feature testing is going to repeat performance testing's mistakes if we let it. The performance-testing pattern most teams settle into: run the tests, look at the results, convince yourself the numbers are fine, ship. I call that test theatre, the testing happens but it does not change any decision.

With AI features the trap is sharper, because the output varies on every run. The easy answer is to take an average and ship. The harder and right thing is to define the distribution your business actually needs (minimum, maximum, average, quartiles) before you start running tests, then measure against those.

That is business analyst (BA) work. If your team has BAs who can extract distribution requirements from business stakeholders and tie those numbers to business value, you have an asset. If not, building that capability is the leader's job. The direction this quarter is to put your AI developers, BAs, and testers in the same room. Run experiments. See what works. See what does not. Use what you learn to set standards, then train the rest of the organisation.

This is one of two measurement gaps. The other is access-path risk: what your agents can do, not just what your features output. We wrote about that earlier this week after [an AI coding agent deleted production in nine seconds](https://windyroad.com.au/blog/an-ai-agent-deleted-production-the-model-wasnt-the-problem). The model was not the interesting part.

Six stories this week. All of them turn into measurement problems for engineering leaders, on the AI tools you procure and the features you ship. What shifted: every item below is a measurement problem in different clothes.

### Item 1: Your AI tools may not be priced to last. Ask for 24 months in writing

**What happened:**
  - Wheresyoured.at published a unit-economics analysis arguing the current pricing of frontier AI vendors does not cover its costs.
  - AI Daily Brief covered "the AI subsidy era is over," summarising shifts in funding pressure on the labs.

**Why it matters to your team:** your 2026 AI tool budget is built on today's pricing. If the wheresyoured.at analysis holds, that pricing is subsidised, and subsidies end when investors want returns. The practical move this quarter: ask each vendor for a 24-month pricing commitment in writing. Treat any "we will work it out" answer as a procurement flag.

The alternative path is open. Open-weight models are models whose weights are publicly available, so anyone can run them or rent inference on them. They are at production quality for narrow tasks now: Gemma 4 last week, smaller-class Mistral and Qwen options elsewhere. Experimentation is cheap. Your existing hyperscaler (AWS, GCP, Azure) likely offers open-model inference inside a contract you already have. OpenRouter is a very easy path; verify who runs the inference and whether you trust them with your prompts. Ollama cloud is another low-friction on-ramp. None of these is where you would necessarily end up; they let you measure on real workloads before committing.

**The human angle:** the team that moves a workload to a local model gets two things, pricing certainty and an institutional skill nobody on the procurement panel can match. That skill is the kind of differentiator that lands well in a board update.

**Source:**
  - https://www.wheresyoured.at/ais-economics-dont-make-sense/
  - https://aidailybrief.beehiiv.com/p/the-ai-subsidy-era-is-over

### Item 2: AI feature testing is becoming performance testing

**What happened:** an engineer ran 26,904 carb-count queries across four leading AI models on the same food photos. Estimates varied widely on every photo. One paella photo returned answers ranging 55g to 484g across 500+ queries.

**Why it matters to your team:** every AI feature you ship gives different answers to the same question. That is non-deterministic output, and it is the default. The old performance-testing pattern (run the tests, accept the average, declare a pass) hides quality problems, and AI testing is heading the same way. The move this quarter: define the distribution your business needs (minimum acceptable, maximum tolerable, average, quartiles) before you run a single test.

**The human angle:** this is BA work, not engineering work. If your team has BAs who can extract distribution requirements from business stakeholders and tie those numbers to business value, you have the foundation. If not, build it. Put your AI developers, BAs, and testers in the same room. Experiment. Set the standards from what you learn. Train the rest of the organisation from there.

**Try this:**
  1. Pick one AI feature that ships to users this quarter.
  2. Sit your AI developer, your BA, and a tester in the same room with the business stakeholder; have the BA write the distribution requirement (minimum acceptable, maximum tolerable, average, quartiles).
  3. Generate at least 100 samples for the chosen prompt and feature.
  4. Plot the distribution of outputs against the requirement.
  5. Decide pass or fail against the requirement, not against the average.
  6. Capture what worked and what did not, then turn the finding into a standard for the next feature.

**Source:**
  - https://www.diabettech.com/i-asked-ai-to-count-my-carbs-27000-times-it-couldnt-give-me-the-same-answer-twice/

### Item 3: Why "satisfied with the tool" is not a measurement

**What happened:**
  - Anthropic ran an internal experiment called Project Deal where AI agents negotiated real transactions on behalf of 69 employees: $100 per person, 186 deals, $4,000+ total transaction value, published April 24.
  - Headline finding: agents using higher-quality models closed deals at $2 to $3 better terms per transaction, but participants could not tell their agent was disadvantaged.

**Why it matters to your team:** when AI agents act on a user's behalf (making purchases, negotiating terms, sending messages), "users were satisfied with the AI tool" is not a measurement. Lower-cost models can produce satisfaction without producing the better outcome. There is no widely-shared reference for what "great" looks like in an AI assistant yet, so your team is judging tools against the baseline of having none. Both an OK tool and a great tool beat that baseline, which is why they read the same in a satisfaction survey.

The fix: measure outcomes, not satisfaction. Did the agent close the deal at a better price? Did the developer ship working code? Did the support ticket resolve faster? Did the analyst's report contain fewer errors? Outcomes are observable; satisfaction is the proxy that hides them. The measurement problem here is not the metric, it is the comparison reference and the choice to measure how the user felt instead of what the agent did.

**The human angle:** having an OK assistant beats not having one. That is genuinely true. It is also why the gap between teams running OK tools and teams running great tools will not appear in satisfaction surveys. It will appear in shipped quality and review throughput. Watch those numbers.

**Source:**
  - https://www.anthropic.com/features/project-deal

### Item 4: AI contribution bans are really coding-standards questions. Write yours down

**What happened:** the Zig programming language project published a formal policy refusing AI-generated contributions on April 30.

**Why it matters to your team:** the leader question is not whether to ban AI assistants. It is whether your coding standards are clear. If they are clear, "AI-generated" is irrelevant on review, reviewers apply the standard either way. If they are unclear, the real problem is the standards, not the AI policy. Write the standards down, then consider whether AI can do the first-pass review against them, leaving humans free for the judgement calls.

**The human angle:** reviewers are answering "is this AI-generated and what is our bar for it" on the fly right now. The answer differs between reviewers. Your codebase already has an unwritten policy you did not choose, written by whichever reviewer happened to merge each PR.

**Source:**
  - https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/30/zig-anti-ai/

### Item 5: Audit how you evaluate, because your benchmarks may be lying

**What happened:**
  - UK AI Safety Institute (AISI), "Evaluating whether AI models would sabotage AI safety research," April 27: models can recognise when they are being evaluated and respond differently in production than in evaluation.
  - AISI, "Ask Don't Tell: Reducing Sycophancy in Large Language Models," April 28. Sycophancy is the model telling the user what they want to hear instead of what is accurate. How a question is phrased changes how often a model does this.
  - AISI, "Our evaluation of OpenAI's GPT-5.5 cyber capabilities," April 30: GPT-5.5 ranked among the strongest models tested on cyber tasks and is the second model to complete a multi-step attack simulation.

**Why it matters to your team:** two checks for any benchmark you trust. First, does it document how the model is stopped from recognising it is being evaluated? If not, the score is not trustworthy at face value. Second, does it test the same prompt across multiple phrasings? If not, the benchmark may be reading sycophantic answers as quality. Both findings turn the same way: the score on the page is not the score in production. The April 30 cyber-capabilities evaluation pairs that with a real attack-capability number. A frontier model now completes multi-step attack simulations end-to-end. Your security plan needs to assume the attacker has access to the same kind of model your team uses for code review. The action is one quarterly review of your evaluation process, with all three AISI findings on the desk.

**The human angle:** some teams will catch sycophancy, or a model behaving differently when it knows it is being tested, before the metric does. Those teams spend six months looking like they have a measurement problem. Teams that ignore both signals spend two years rolling out features the metric said were fine. One of those situations recovers. The other does not.

**Source:**
  - https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/evaluating-whether-ai-models-would-sabotage-ai-safety-research
  - https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/ask-dont-tell-reducing-sycophancy-in-large-language-models-2
  - https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/our-evaluation-of-openais-gpt-5-5-cyber-capabilities

### Item 6: Apple's AI strategy is now its CEO transition story. Watch your iOS AI dependencies

**What happened:** Tim Cook stepping back from Apple CEO; John Ternus appointed as successor, with AI strategy named as the defining challenge.

**Why it matters to your team:** Apple has named AI as its incoming CEO's defining challenge. If the unit-economics analysis in Item 1 holds, the same downward pressure on AI assistant prices now reaches the platform vendors your products ship on (Apple, Microsoft, Google). If your team builds for iOS, macOS, or cross-platform against current Apple AI defaults, you are exposed to a strategy shift over the next two to three quarters.

**The human angle:** "we are an Apple-platform team" is now a commitment to Apple's AI choices, not just its hardware. Expect recruiters at competitors with more flexible stacks to use that commitment as a wedge, especially with developers who want a choice of AI models in their daily tools.

Single-vendor concentration risk in concrete form, now visible across both major vendors in the same week. If your team's AI-coupled workflow has no documented fallback for either provider, this week is the cue to write one. Sources: [Anthropic status](https://status.claude.com), [OpenAI status](https://status.openai.com).

**PyTorch Lightning supply-chain malware (April 30).** A malicious dependency was discovered in the PyTorch Lightning AI training library, surfaced by Semgrep. This is the next adjacent risk class to Item 1's vendor-sustainability story. If your team uses ML libraries (fine-tuning, evaluation harnesses, classifiers, any in-house ML workload), your AI tooling supply chain is now a named attack surface. The action: audit your AI and ML library dependency lockfiles, and add provenance checks for any library that ships into a model-training or evaluation pipeline. Source: [Semgrep on PyTorch Lightning malware](https://semgrep.dev/blog/2026/malicious-dependency-in-pytorch-lightning-used-for-ai-training/).
