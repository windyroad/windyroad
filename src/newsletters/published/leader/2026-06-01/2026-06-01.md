# Issue 07: The AI pilot phase is over. The discipline phase has started.

*The Shift: AI engineering, week ending 2026-05-31*

**From Tom**

For most of the past two years, AI has been a pilot-shaped purchase: small budgets, easy to experiment with, ROI loosely defined, security tolerated as 'we will figure it out', governance deferred to next quarter. This week three things landed in the same five days that retire that mode. Corporate buyers started rationing the spend openly, and AI's loudest voices walked back their own jobs forecasts. The first vendor-published frameworks for evaluating AI models shipped. A real-world supply-chain vulnerability in the Python stack underpinning most AI services made the patch list. Three moments that move AI from discovery to governance, all in the same week. The new default for AI procurement is discipline. The question for engineering leaders this quarter is no longer 'is the AI ready' but 'do we have the discipline to keep it running once it is'.

### Item 1: Anthropic at $965B. Customers are starting to ration AI.

**What happened:**

- Anthropic [closed a $65 billion Series H at a $965 billion post-money valuation](https://www.anthropic.com/news/series-h) on 28 May, with [WSJ](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Anthropic+Rockets+to+%24965+Billion+Valuation%22+%22Topping+OpenAI%22+WSJ) and [NYT](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Anthropic+Tops+OpenAI%22+%22Most+Valuable%22+New+York+Times) framing it as Anthropic topping OpenAI to become the world's most valuable AI startup.
- WSJ reported ["Corporate America Is Starting to Ration AI as Cost Skyrockets"](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/corporate-america-is-starting-to-ration-ai-as-cost-skyrockets-1eb99d7a) on 29 May.
- Axios called it ["AI sticker shock"](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/28/ai-spending-roi-enterprise-costs) and reported [CEOs "bargain hunting for AI"](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22CEOs+go+bargain+hunting+for+AI%22+axios) on 28 to 30 May.
- Apollo [shopped a $36 billion debt deal to buy Google chips for Anthropic](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Apollo+Shops+%2436+Billion+Debt+Deal%22+Anthropic+Bloomberg) on 28 May; SoftBank committed [up to €75 billion to French AI centres](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22SoftBank+Plans+Up+to%22+French+AI+Centers+Bloomberg) on 30 May.

**Why it matters to your team:** At $965 billion, Anthropic's valuation now sits above almost every traditional enterprise software vendor your team buys from. The same customers funding that valuation are pulling back on AI usage in the same week.

**The human angle:** The cost of using AI is now a budget question, not just a cloud-spend question. The rationing news pushes per-call economics from cloud-bill line items into product decisions. The next budget review will ask which AI features are paying for themselves and which are being subsidised by the rest of the product.

### Item 2: Patch your Python AI servers this week

**What happened:**

- [Ars Technica](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Millions+of+AI+agents+imperiled%22+Ars+Technica+Starlette) reported on 26 May that a critical vulnerability in the Starlette web framework exposes millions of AI agents to authentication bypass.
- Starlette is the Python async server library underpinning FastAPI, vLLM, and many Model Context Protocol server implementations.
- The Belgian [Centre for Cybersecurity](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Starlette+framework%22+Belgium+FastAPI+authentication+bypass) issued a separate advisory on 28 May.
- [GIGAZINE](https://news.google.com/search?q=GIGAZINE+Starlette+%22300+million+times+a+week%22+AI+agents) noted Starlette is downloaded more than 300 million times a week; [Techzine](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Vulnerability+in+open-source+component+puts+AI+platforms+at+risk%22+Techzine) covered the platform impact on 27 May.

**Why it matters to your team:** If your team runs any Python AI service, Starlette is in your stack directly or via FastAPI. The patch is owed before the next deployment cycle.

**Operational steps this week:**

1. Identify Starlette in your stack: `pip show starlette` plus a transitive scan via `pip-audit` or `safety check`.
2. Pin the patched Starlette version in your lockfile and rebuild any container image that ships a Python AI service.
3. Add an authenticated-endpoint smoke test to the deployment gate so the same regression cannot reappear silently.

**The human angle:** A Software Bill of Materials for AI-stack dependencies is the artefact the next audit will ask for. If yours does not yet cover the Python AI surface, the patch is a natural moment to extend it.

### Item 3: Altman and Amodei walked back AI jobs predictions. Public sentiment is what changed.

**What happened:**

- Axios reported [billionaires work to contain AI's populist revolt](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22the+pitchforks+are+here%22+Axios+billionaires+AI+populist+revolt), framed as "the pitchforks are here", on 30 May.
- Fortune reported [Sam Altman and Dario Amodei "walking back AI jobs-apocalypse prophecies"](https://fortune.com/2026/05/26/sam-altman-dario-amodei-walking-back-ai-jobs-apocalypse-prophecies-ipo/) on 26 May.
- Australia's Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC) reported [Australians' growing privacy concerns paired with declining trust in AI](https://www.oaic.gov.au/news/media-centre/australians-more-concerned-about-privacy-as-trust-in-ai-languishes,-survey-finds) on 28 May, citing the Australian Community Attitudes to Privacy Survey (ACAPS).

**Why it matters to your team:** Public sentiment is moving against the case for AI-driven headcount cuts, and the loudest AI voices are quietly recalibrating their own jobs forecasts. Any internal narrative that frames headcount savings as the win is now exposed.

**The human angle:** The audience for an AI deployment narrative has widened beyond the all-hands. The same wording is now read by journalists, regulators, and a workforce watching the public sentiment shift. Any forward statement is worth pressure-testing against that wider audience.

### Item 4: US federal AI direction is gone. EU rules set the binding clock.

**What happened:**

- Politico documented an explicit White House split: ["Inside the White House's AI split"](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Inside+the+White+House%27s+AI+split%22+Politico) on 28 May, plus ["Factions inside the Trump administration wrestle over how to handle AI"](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Factions+inside+the+Trump+administration+wrestle%22+AI+Politico) the same day.
- The administration previously [pulled an AI executive order](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Trump+yanked+AI+order%22+David+Sacks+Politico) after David Sacks raised industry concerns, on 21 May.
- Axios reported [the Democratic resistance on AI](https://news.google.com/search?q=%22Inside+the+Democratic+resistance+on+AI%22+Axios) on 29 May.
- The EU AI Act's [Article 50 transparency rules](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/transparency-rules-article-50/) carry a 2 August 2026 commencement, less than three months from now.

**Why it matters to your team:** The regulatory clock is not the US federal clock this cycle. If your team ships to EU users, Californians, or Australians, those calendars are the dates that move, and right now only EU Article 50 sits inside the next-quarter planning horizon. US state regulators and Australian regulators are active but not yet setting binding AI calendars; the EU is.

**Operational steps this quarter:**

1. Inventory which jurisdictions your end-users sit in. The EU bucket is the only one with a binding AI-rule date inside the next 90 days.
2. Calendar EU AI Act Article 50 transparency requirements against your release plan: which user-facing AI surfaces need disclosure changes before 2 August 2026?
3. Drop US-federal-direction-dependent line items from your AI compliance roadmap. The US federal direction is not arriving this cycle; the line items are baseline carrying cost until it does.

**The human angle:** A compliance posture anchored to US federal direction is anchored to the slowest clock in the room, and the clock stopped advancing. For compliance leads who built the quarterly roadmap around expected US federal guidance, that is a real rework moment: line items move from 'wait for direction' to 'commit to the EU calendar and watch US state and Australian signals'. The cognitive-load shift is that the team now tracks three regulatory rhythms (binding, forming, signalling) instead of one.

### Item 5: Claude is on 276,000 KPMG desks. The AI procurement question changed this week.

**What happened:**

- Anthropic [integrated Claude across KPMG's 276,000-plus workforce](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-kpmg) on 19 May.
- Anthropic [expanded the PwC partnership](https://www.anthropic.com/news/pwc-expanded-partnership) for technology building and deal execution on 14 May.
- OpenAI [partnered with Dell to bring Codex to hybrid and on-premises enterprise environments](https://openai.com/index/dell-codex-enterprise-partnership/) on 18 May.

**Why it matters to your team:** The procurement question is no longer "which model do we license" but "whose consulting team will run the integration, and what are the exit terms".

**The human angle:** Teams used to learn a new tool from the vendor's documentation. Now they learn it from a consulting-partner-trained engineer in the next chair. New joiners pick up those patterns first. Over time the consulting-partner view of 'how Claude works here' codifies as the team's view. That is a quiet status shift for whoever built the team's first Claude integration. Two questions are worth asking early. Who owns the relationship with the consulting partner? When does the team escalate to the vendor rather than working it out internally?

### Item 6: OpenAI published two frameworks for evaluating AI models. The build-it-yourself starting point just changed.

**What happened:**

- OpenAI published its [Frontier Governance Framework](https://openai.com/index/openai-frontier-governance-framework/) on 28 May.
- OpenAI published [A shared playbook for trustworthy third-party evaluations](https://openai.com/index/trustworthy-third-party-evaluations-foundations/) on 29 May.
- Both join the [UK AI Safety Institute (AISI) body of evaluation work](https://www.aisi.gov.uk/work) as published references for how a major AI vendor or regulator evaluates frontier AI models.

**Why it matters to your team:** If your team had been waiting for a reference framework before building its own AI-model evaluation infrastructure, two have now landed in the last month. The case to fund that work this quarter rather than next is stronger than it was last week.

**The human angle:** Teams that have been waiting for a published framework to anchor evaluation work now have two of them. For whoever built the team's first ad-hoc eval harness, that is a moment of mixed feelings. The work was useful and the vendor-published framework now sets the reference. The internal decision to fund shared evaluation infrastructure no longer rests on inventing the structure from first principles. Quarterly governance reviews now have two vendor frameworks to weigh against the team's own results. The synthesis work shifts from build-the-harness to choose-what-to-keep, which is faster but harder to defend without an explicit decision record.

### Item 7: Anthropic published how it isolates Claude when running code. A reference for any team doing the same.

**What happened:**

- Simon Willison [documented Anthropic's published explanation of how it isolates Claude across its products](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/30/how-we-contain-claude/) on 30 May.
- The approach covers Claude.ai, Claude Code, and Cowork using four different isolation technologies (gVisor, macOS Seatbelt, Linux Bubblewrap, and full virtual machines), chosen by workload.

**Why it matters to your team:** If your team runs coding agents on untrusted code, you now have a major-AI-vendor reference for how to isolate them rather than building blind.

**The human angle:** For security architects who set the team's sandbox strategy before a vendor reference existed, this is mixed terrain. Validation where the team's choices match Anthropic's approach. An audit-trail prompt where they do not. The next security review's question shifts from 'what should we use' to 'why does our choice differ from Anthropic's, and is the difference deliberate'. That is a different conversation, and it lands in a meeting that already has limited time.

Revenue (not just valuation) is concentrating in a single AI vendor on a five-month cadence, which makes vendor-financial-stability and exit-clause attention part of next-quarter procurement work.
