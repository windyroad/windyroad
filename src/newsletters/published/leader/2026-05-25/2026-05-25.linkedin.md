## LinkedIn Post

Most promises that AI will shrink your headcount are overconfident. Here is the mechanism.

AI's real prize is capacity, not a smaller team. (The Shift, Issue 06)

Every business process has a bottleneck: the one step that sets the pace. A process only speeds up if you speed up that step. But AI gets pointed at whatever is easiest to automate, which is rarely the bottleneck. So the efficiency the headcount case rests on often never arrives, and when it does, the constraint just moves to the next step.

Three stories this week all turned on it:

1. The cost case got shakier. Fortune reported AI's run-cost can top the staff it replaces, and Uber burned its entire 2026 AI budget in four months. HSBC and Standard Chartered went public on AI and jobs, with one CEO forced to apologise for the "lower-value human capital" framing. The prize is usually more capacity from the same team, not the same output from fewer people.

2. More review will not fix AI slop. The Wall Street Journal reported two engineers behind the agentic-coding boom warning of a "vibe slop crisis," where AI pumps out code that hardens into technical debt. Left alone, an AI codebase decays toward slop, the way any system decays without energy put back in. Human review still matters, but it cannot scale to AI's volume, and gating every change behind a person caps AI at human speed. The fix is automated counter-pressure in the pipeline.

3. AI now finds vulnerabilities faster than anyone can patch them. Anthropic's Project Glasswing found more than 10,000 high or critical bugs in a month; only 75 of 530 disclosed are patched, about two weeks each. The gain is finding; the bottleneck is patching.
