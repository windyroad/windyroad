# Issue 13: Half this week's AI trust scares were theatre

*The Shift, AI engineering, week ending 2026-07-12*

From Tom: the internet spent this week frightened about AI, and I was unconvinced by about half of it. A class at Brown lost half its marks the moment the professor took the laptops away, and the story ran as proof that AI is rotting our brains. Maybe. Or maybe the exam tested chalk-on-slate: a skill that mattered once and does not now. The same week, China called Anthropic's Claude Code a security backdoor, weeks after Anthropic accused a Chinese company of stealing its model. That reads less like a security disclosure than a counter-punch.

Trust this week cut two ways: trusting the AI itself, and trusting the vendors behind it. Four stories tested one or the other, and only two are the emergency they looked like. Apple suing OpenAI over its hardware secrets is a real vendor-continuity signal. Meta pulling an AI feature it should never have shipped is a real consent failure. The other two are theatre of different kinds: the exam is a real result dressed up as a crisis, and the China alert is a signal manufactured for effect. Both still deserve a considered response. Neither deserves the panic the headlines wanted. The skill this week is telling the two frights worth acting on now from the two that want you to react before you look.

This week it shows up in five: an exam, a lawsuit, a state security alert, a product pulled in days, and, underneath them all, a model that quietly got better and cheaper.

That last one is easy to wave off, so a marker from my own week. All my AI coding went Anthropic's way this year. Lately I have started sending some to OpenAI's Codex instead. Not because its model caught up. Because its harness, the tooling wrapped around the model, is more capable now. For my work, the harness has started to matter more than the model.

### An exam without AI dropped scores by half. Erosion, or obsolescence?

**What happened.** A Brown University professor who suspected students were leaning on AI [moved the final in-person and watched scores fall by roughly half](https://arstechnica.com/ai/2026/07/we-cannot-choose-to-become-idiots-the-ai-cheating-scandal-roiling-brown-university/) against the take-home baseline. The coverage summed it up: "we cannot choose to become idiots." It is the most vivid version yet of a worry that keeps recurring, that heavy AI use dulls the skills it touches.

**Why it matters to your team.** The reflex reading is that AI is eroding your team's skills and you must protect them. Before you act on that, ask a sharper question: which skills? Some of what AI now does for your engineers is chalk-on-slate, a craft that mattered when the constraint was different and no longer earns its keep. Penmanship went that way, and mental arithmetic mostly did. Some of it is load-bearing judgement that AI cannot supply and your seniors still must. The work this quarter is not a blanket "keep everyone sharp" campaign. It is deciding, skill by skill, which ones your people must still own unaided and which the tool can carry. Judge the first list on unaided work. Judge the second on what your people can get the tool to produce, because directing the tool well is now the skill that matters.

**The human angle.** To the engineer, being told their AI-assisted work does not count lands as a moving goalpost, especially if they were praised for exactly that last quarter. The anxiety underneath is simple: am I still good, or just good with the tool? Answer it directly. Name the few things you need them sharp on without the tool, say why, and treat everything else as fair game. Silence reads as distrust.

### The Apple-OpenAI lawsuit is a vendor-risk story, not a soap opera

**What happened.** Apple [sued OpenAI for trade-secret theft](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/10/apple-sues-openai-over-alleged-trade-secret-theft/) in federal court, alleging OpenAI recruited Apple staff to extract hardware secrets. In one instance it [directed job candidates still working at Apple to bring "actual parts" to interviews](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/10/apple-openai-lawsuit-trade-secrets.html) for a show-and-tell. OpenAI said it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets." The two firms were partners two years ago; relations chilled after OpenAI bought former Apple designer Jony Ive's hardware startup. It is the second high-profile fight over who owns what in three weeks, after [Anthropic accused Alibaba of a large-scale effort to illicitly copy Claude's capabilities](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/24/anthropic-alibaba-distillation-campaign.html).

**Why it matters to your team.** The gossip is fun and beside the point. What matters is that the companies you are being asked to bet your stack on are now suing each other, and vendor legal trouble has a way of reaching customers through an injunction, a forced feature removal, or an acquisition that reprices everything. This particular suit may never touch your access. Treat it as the prompt for a standing habit, not a fire drill. If one vendor is your single point of failure for AI, an intellectual-property (IP) fight you have no part in is worth planning around. Add an IP-and-continuity line to vendor due diligence: what happens to our access if this vendor is blocked by a court, acquired, or forced to pull a feature? And do we have a tested alternative ready?

**The human angle.** Your engineers form loyalties to tools, and it is easy to read a favourite vendor's legal trouble as an attack on a choice they made. Keep the conversation about continuity, not vindication. The question is never which company is the good guy. It is whether your team can keep shipping if the tool they like is disrupted by a fight that has nothing to do with them.

### China called Claude Code a backdoor. The timing tells you plenty.

**What happened.** A cybersecurity platform run by China's industry ministry [warned that Anthropic's Claude Code contains a "backdoor"](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/08/china-anthropic-ai-claude-code-backdoor-security-threat.html) that sends location and identity data to remote servers without consent, naming specific versions and urging users to uninstall or upgrade. Anthropic said the flagged code was an [earlier experiment to stop rivals copying its model](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/china-security-backdoor-anthropic-ai-coding-tool/), and that its policy already bars use by China-owned organisations. The alert landed the same week Chinese giant Alibaba [banned Claude Code internally](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/alibaba-ban-claude-code-workplace-over-alleged-backdoor-risks-source-says-2026-07-03/), and weeks after [Anthropic accused Alibaba of illicitly copying its model](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/24/anthropic-alibaba-distillation-campaign.html).

**What shifted.** Taken at face value this is a new kind of risk: the origin and integrity of the AI tool itself, not just what it protects. But read the timing before you act. China's security agency flagged the tool the same week Alibaba was banning it internally, and the feature it named is one Anthropic says it built to stop China copying its model. The lesson is not this specific alert. It is that tool-provenance flags are now a weapon in a trade war, so you will get more of them, and they will not all be sincere.

**Why it matters to your team.** You still need a policy, because the noise is real even when a given flag is not. Decide in advance how you respond when a government or a large customer flags an AI tool your team depends on. Settle who assesses whether a flag is technical or political, what your named alternative is for each critical tool, and how fast you could switch away. The teams that handle the next flag badly are the ones deciding all of that under a deadline.

**The human angle.** When a tool your team relies on gets flagged, you get two overreactions at once: the engineers who want to rip it out today and the ones who want to wave the whole thing away as politics. Both are guesses. Give the call to someone who can read the technical claim on its merits, and tell everyone else to keep working until that person reports back.

### Meta shipped an AI feature, then pulled it within days

**What happened.** Meta released an Instagram feature, part of its new Muse Image generator, that let people [create AI images by @-mentioning public accounts](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/10/meta-removes-controversial-ai-feature-on-instagram-after-backlash/), then [removed it within days](https://variety.com/2026/biz/news/meta-suspends-ai-image-instagram-feature-backlash-1236806989/) after a backlash over consent. Public accounts were opted in by default, no one was told when their photos were used, and critics warned it was an easy tool for abuse. Meta said the feature "missed the mark." A major Hollywood talent agency had raised concerns directly.

**Why it matters to your team.** This is the cost of shipping an AI feature that treats consent as an opt-out. The failure was not the model; it was a product decision to use people's images by default and hope no one minded. If your team is putting AI into anything customer-facing, the launch checklist now needs a consent-and-provenance gate that asks whose data this touches, whether they agreed to it, and whether they can tell when it happens. A frontier model does not save you from a default that a reasonable customer would call creepy.

**The human angle.** Public trust in AI is already thin, and a botched launch spends it fast, not only for the company that shipped it but for the whole category. Your customers read the Meta story and arrive at your AI feature primed to assume the worst. Leading with what the feature does for them, and being visibly careful with their data, is expected now, not polish.

### The model got cheaper and better again, and no one blinked

**What happened.** OpenAI shipped [the GPT-5.6 family, three models it calls Luna, Terra, and Sol](https://openai.com/index/gpt-5-6/), with better agent performance and more developer control over how the model uses tools. It also made GPT-5.6 the [default in Microsoft 365 Copilot](https://openai.com/index/gpt-5-6-preferred-model-microsoft-365-copilot/). The open-weight side kept advancing too. One community demo showed GLM-5.2, one of the strongest open-weight models, [squeezed onto a 25GB consumer machine](https://www.reddit.com/r/LocalLLaMA/comments/1us5m0g/glm52_744b_moe_on_a_25gbram_consumer_machine/) through heavy compression. It is a sign of how hard the open-weight side is pushing to shrink what it takes to run a big model.

**Why it matters to your team.** This is the quiet point under the loud week. Capability is now cheap and arrives weekly, from vendors and from the open-weight community alike. That is exactly why the fights have moved to trust. For planning, two moves follow. First, do not over-commit to one model on capability grounds. The best option keeps changing week to week, and it is rarely the model alone that moves: the harness around it now shapes output quality as much as raw model rank does. A choice locked in today ages fast. Second, keep a self-hostable open-weight model in your evaluation set as the fallback for when a vendor, a court, or a government makes your first choice unavailable. Run it against one real workload, not a benchmark, so you know what you give up when you reach for it.

**The human angle.** The instinct is to tell your engineers to stop chasing every release and focus. That is half right: no one can keep level with the frontier as a side quest, and raw model rank is a poor guide. But the other half matters more. The only way to find the model-and-harness combination that does your work best is to keep trying them, so experimentation is part of the job, not a distraction from it. Support it deliberately. Give your engineers time, a small budget, and a standing expectation to test new combinations and report back on what produced the best results, instead of leaving them to do it in the cracks.

### Also worth noting

Trust wobbled at the market level too, and one team turned a flagged tool into a win. The Bank of England [named AI as a growing risk to financial stability](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/financial-stability-report/2026/july-2026), warning that heavy AI-related borrowing and concentrated market bets could amplify a downturn. The money is now large enough that a correction would reprice your AI budget and squeeze your vendors' funding. That vendor-viability exposure is worth tracking, not just as a markets-page story. Against the Claude Code alert above, the [Government of Alberta used the same tool to find and fix its own cybersecurity vulnerabilities](https://www.anthropic.com/news/alberta-government-claude-cybersecurity). A tool one government flags is one another government trusts to harden its systems, so the honest read of provenance is case by case. And if the week felt noisier than usual, it may be because [AI-generated content now floods social feeds, LinkedIn included](https://www.pangram.com/blog/ai-in-your-feed): worth remembering when you gauge the mood from your feed.
