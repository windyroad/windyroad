The models got cheaper and better again this week. Nobody argued about that.

Every fight was about trust instead, and half of them do not survive a second look.

The Shift, Issue 13: Half this week's AI trust scares were theatre.

Apple sued OpenAI. China called Claude Code a backdoor. An in-person exam cut scores in half. Meta pulled an AI feature after a consent backlash. Two are real, two are theatre.

Real: Apple v OpenAI is a vendor-continuity signal, not a soap opera. If one vendor is your single point of failure, add an IP-and-continuity line to your due diligence.

Also real: Meta's consent-by-default feature. If you ship AI to customers, gate the launch on consent before it goes out.

Theatre: China's Claude Code backdoor alert landed the same week Alibaba banned the tool, and weeks after Anthropic accused Alibaba of copying its model. Treat tool-provenance flags as trade-war weapons; they will not all be sincere.

Also theatre: the exam. Scores were real, but "AI is rotting brains" is the wrong read. Decide which skills your team must keep unaided, and stop grading them on the skills the tool now carries.

Underneath it all: capability is cheap and weekly now, and the tooling wrapped around the model matters as much as the model. Make experimentation a supported, budgeted part of the job.

Which trust story this week are you changing a decision over, and which are you letting pass?
