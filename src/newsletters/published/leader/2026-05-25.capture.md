# Capture transcript: The Shift, week ending 2026-05-24

## Item 1: The cost reckoning (lead)

- **Outcome**: Adjust
- **Original take presented**: First sustained pushback on the vendor-led capability-commoditisation drift; the move for leaders is to require any AI initiative pitched on headcount savings to carry a loaded-cost-and-throughput line before approval.
- **Source**: https://fortune.com/2026/05/22/microsoft-ai-cost-problem-tokens-agents/ ; https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/20/intuit-to-lay-off-over-3000-employees-to-refocus-on-ai/
- **Adjust text** (Tom, verbatim):

  > Agree, but more nuanced. We're too early in the innovation cycle. No-one knows what the real impact will be from these initiatives. We can hope for a reduced head-count need, but anyone who's promising it is over confident. We need to test and learn. On top of that, head-count reduction is often the wrong answer. When AI is being used to improve a process, we need to understand that what really happens is that it just moves the bottle neck. We need to learn what the next bottleneck is and then see how we can optimise that. Again a test and learn activity. Finally, depending on the process, the real desire might be to increase capacity rather than reduce head-count while maintaining capacity. What value does the process produce for the business - can increasing capacity or reducing cycle time, increase value further. Concrete example: for many businesses, IT capacity provides very big restrictions on what the business can achieve and improve with their IT - this becomes a boat anchor for the business. IT efficiency improvements using AI being turned into head count reduction reduces the costs, but leaves the low capacity in place resulting in IT remaining a boat anchor.
